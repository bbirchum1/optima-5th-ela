Johnny Tremain
⚒️ Week 8 · Fluency

This is the last week of Johnny Tremain. Rab has died. The war has begun. Johnny stands on Lexington Green, not knowing yet what comes next, and then he hears it: a drum far away, growing closer. This passage is the ending of the novel. Read it as if you know what it cost to get here—and what it means for what comes next.

This Week’s Fluency Passage — Chapter 12
He felt free, light, unreal, and utterly alone … Tomorrow—next day—it would be different, but today is today.

Then far away, but coming nearer and nearer down along Menotomy road, he heard the throb of a drum. Men coming back from Charlestown. He stood, turned his head to listen. The shuffle of feet. A fife began to toot. It was ill-played. Maybe a foolish tune, but Johnny warmed to hear it. For once—once more—Yankee Doodle was going to town.

True, Rab had died. Hundreds would die, but not the thing they died for.
— Esther Forbes, Johnny Tremain, Chapter 12

🗺️ Your Fluency Path
1
Listen
Watch the video. Notice the three distinct sections: the still, alone feeling of the first paragraph; the arrival of the drum in the second; and the final three-word sentence at the end. Listen for how each part has a completely different tone and pace.
2
Read for Accuracy
Read slowly. Pay attention to: utterly, Menotomy, Charlestown, Yankee Doodle. The ellipsis after “alone” means pause. The dashes in “Tomorrow—next day” are short, sharp pauses. Don’t skip them.
3
Read for Smoothness
Read again and let the rhythm build. The drum is “far away, but coming nearer and nearer.” Let your voice do that—start quiet and build. The final line should land on its own: separate, final, unforgettable. Don’t rush past it.
4
Read for Expression
Read this passage as if you know everything that came before it. Rab is gone. The war has started. Johnny is alone on the Green. When the drum begins, something lifts. Let that feeling come through your voice. The last sentence is not sad—it is a statement of fact and a declaration of faith. Read it that way.
5
Record and Submit
When you are ready, record your reading and upload to Canvas. This is the last page of a great American novel. Read it like you mean it.
✅ Fluency Self-Check
🎯Did I pause correctly at the ellipsis and the dashes?
🥁Did my voice actually build as the drum came nearer?
🎭Did the final sentence — “True, Rab had died. Hundreds would die, but not the thing they died for” — land as its own complete moment?
💛Did I read this as if I cared about what happened to Johnny and Rab?

Daily Oral Language
Tense revision — fix the shift in the passage

Sentence 1 — The Correct Model

While Nat studied the almanac by candlelight, he found hundreds of errors in the navigation tables.

Sentence 2 — Fix It

while nat checked moores almanac he notices that many of the calculations are wrong and writes them all down

Corrected: While Nat checked Moore’s Almanac, he noticed that many of the calculations were wrong and wrote them all down.

Fixes: “Nat” capitalized; “Moore’s” (proper noun + possessive); comma after the subordinate clause; “notices” → “noticed” and “are” → “were” and “writes” → “wrote” (tense shifts corrected to past).

Grammar · Day 3
Using Consistent Tense in Informational Writing
🎬
Coming soon

This paragraph about Nat Bowditch has multiple tense shift errors. Read it carefully, identify every error, then rewrite the whole paragraph in consistent past tense in your reading journal.

Nat Bowditch worked long hours at the chandlery during the day. At night, he opens his books and studies by candlelight. He teaches himself Latin, French, and advanced mathematics. When he finally obtains a copy of Moore’s Almanac, he checked every calculation carefully. He finds hundreds of errors that no one had noticed before. He writes them all down in a notebook.

Corrected (all past tense): Nat Bowditch worked long hours at the chandlery during the day. At night, he opened his books and studied by candlelight. He taught himself Latin, French, and advanced mathematics. When he finally obtained a copy of Moore’s Almanac, he checked every calculation carefully. He found hundreds of errors that no one had noticed before. He wrote them all down in a notebook.

Six verbs needed correcting. Every shift broke the reader’s sense of time. In informational writing about a historical figure, consistent past tense keeps the reader grounded.

Write your revised paragraph in your reading journal.

What Good Readers Do
Notice when an author shows precision in action

In Chapter 7, Latham shows us what extraordinary precision looks like — not by telling us Nat was a genius, but by showing us exactly what he did. Good readers notice when an author demonstrates something instead of just naming it.

🧠 Watch for this

What is Moore’s Almanac, and why do sailors depend on it?

How does Nat discover the errors? What does his process of checking reveal about how his mind works?

How does Latham make the number of errors feel significant to the reader?

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex now plans Body 2: making knowledge accessible. Four candidates from Chs. 5–7.

BEFORE
Alex’s options: (1) Corrects Moore’s Navigator errors. (2) Teaches sailors navigation by logarithms. (3) Calls it ‘a simple matter of mathematics.’ (4) Sam Smith says only weaklings give up when becalmed.
AFTER
Alex’s ranking for Body 2: (1) Correcting Moore’s — concrete achievement with stakes (sailors’ lives). (2) Teaching sailors — shows the ‘accessible to anyone’ part of the claim directly. Cuts: The Sam quote — important for Body 1 (self-made), less relevant here. The ‘simple matter’ quote — great but goes in Body 2 as a capstone, not evidence.

Notice: The ‘simple matter’ quote is now planned as a capstone detail inside Body 2, not the main evidence. Evidence ≠ examples. Evidence is what PROVES your subtopic.

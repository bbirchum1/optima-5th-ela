Chapter 2 Question
What is cleverness, exactly?

Praiseworthy doesn’t catch the thief with a trap. He catches him with psychology. He understands what the thief fears, and he uses that fear. Is this the same as wisdom? Is it cunning? Chapter 2 answers the question: what is Praiseworthy really good at?

Sentence 2 — Fix It

This sentence has a punctuation error and a word choice problem. Find both and rewrite it in your reading journal.

praiseworthy stepped forward calm as you please and said his plan was very simple.

Corrected: Praiseworthy stepped forward, as unconcerned as you please, and said his plan was very simple.

Fixes: Capitalize Praiseworthy; add commas to set off the parenthetical “as unconcerned as you please”; replace calm with unconcerned (the word Fleischman uses, which adds irony — Praiseworthy isn’t just calm, he seems completely unaware there’s any problem).

When you see an unfamiliar word, check for known roots. If you see aud → it has something to do with hearing. If you see or → speaking or mouth.

Read: Chapter 2 — “How to Catch a Thief”
A pig, a coal-dust trick, and one word that says everything
📌 Reading Focus

Chapter 2 is a heist story — but the best kind, where the hero wins not through force but through insight into human psychology. The sow doesn’t need to squeal. The trick is already done before the lights go out. As you read, ask yourself: at what point does Praiseworthy know he has the thief?

📖 Read or Listen

Read Chapter 2 in your novel.

⏸️ Pause: The Sow and the Plan

Praiseworthy tells the passengers the sow will squeal if a dishonest man touches her. But that’s not really the plan at all. Why does the pig need to be there?

📓
⏸️ Pause: “Humbug”

When Cut-Eye Higgins is caught, he says they’ll meet again. Praiseworthy responds: “Humbug.” Based on context, what do you think this word means?

What Good Readers Do
Understand how mystery and comedy overlap

Chapter 2 is technically a mystery: there’s a thief, a plan, a reveal. But it’s also comedy: the plan involves a pig covered in coal dust, a room full of gold-seekers touching a hog in the dark, and a “judge” with a cigar. What Fleischman does brilliantly is let you enjoy both at the same time. The comedy doesn’t undermine the mystery — it amplifies it. By the time Praiseworthy says “Humbug,” you feel both the satisfaction of the puzzle and the delight of the absurdity.

📖 Reading Question 1
Explain Praiseworthy’s plan to catch the thief. What was the pig actually for? Why didn’t the pig need to squeal?
📓
📖 Reading Question 2
Who is Cut-Eye Higgins, and what does he say to Jack and Praiseworthy when he’s caught? What does his response tell us about him as a character?
📓
📖 Reading Question 3
Praiseworthy says “Humbug” to Cut-Eye Higgins. Based on context, what do you think this word means? Why is it the perfect word for Praiseworthy to choose at this moment?

Chapter 2 showed you something powerful: the plot twist. Praiseworthy’s plan wasn’t what it seemed. The pig was a fake-out. Good adventure stories often have moments where the apparent plan turns out to be cover for a deeper plan. Does your story have a moment like this? Could it?

Morphology · Introduction
Morphemes — The Building Blocks of Words
🌱
Teacher introduces morphemes and word parts

This week you learned that morphemes are the smallest parts of a word that carry meaning. Prefixes like dis- and un- change meaning. Suffixes like -ly and -ment change how a word works in a sentence.

📌 This Week's Word Parts

disapproval — dis (not) + approv (to favor) + al (noun suffix)

uncomfortable — un (not) + comfort (ease) + able (capable of)

impatiently — im (not) + patient (able to wait) + ly (in a way)

respectably — respect (to honor) + able (worthy of) + ly (in a way)

Grammar · Explain
Why Complete Sentences Matter
✏️
Teacher explains why writers need complete sentences

All week you have been working with complete sentences, fragments, and run-ons. A complete sentence has a subject and a predicate and expresses a whole thought.

👉 From the novel

Complete: “No one in my family has any use for such frippery.” — This is a complete sentence. Matthew (the subject) makes a clear, forceful statement (the predicate).

Fragment: “Five slits in the sleeves.” — This is a fragment. There is no verb. But the author uses it on purpose, to capture Judith's shock.

Writers use complete sentences to make their ideas clear. Sometimes authors use fragments deliberately for effect, but they know the rules first.

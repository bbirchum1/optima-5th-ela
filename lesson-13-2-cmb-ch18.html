What Good Readers Do
Identify figurative language and explain its function

Chapter 18 is a chapter of action — the Astrea rushes to rescue a vessel in distress. But Latham doesn’t write this as a simple adventure scene. She uses figurative language to make the reader feel the urgency, the darkness, the cold spray of the sea. Good readers identify specific figurative language and explain what work it is doing in the text.

🧠 Watch for this

When Latham writes about the rescue, what figurative language does she use to convey urgency or danger? Identify at least one specific example as you read.

How does Nat’s mathematical precision contribute to the rescue? Where does his skill intersect with the moment of crisis?

This chapter shows Nat in an emergency. How does the figurative language Latham uses help convey what is at stake?

Sentence 2 — Fix It

Find every mistake and write the corrected sentence in your reading journal.

the sailors who trusted nat knew that his calculations which had never failed him would transfer their ship safely through the dangerous passage

Corrected: The sailors who trusted Nat knew that his calculations, which had never failed him, would transfer their ship safely through the dangerous passage.

Fixes: capitalize “The” and “Nat”; commas around non-restrictive “which had never failed him”; period at end.

Poetry Corner
A poem about skill meeting the moment of crisis

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow published “The Wreck of the Hesperus” in 1842 — a dramatic poem about a sea disaster caused by ignoring a warning. Read these opening stanzas today, as Nat’s Astrea races to rescue a ship in distress.

📜 Poetry Corner
“The Wreck of the Hesperus” (opening)
Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, 1842 — public domain
It was the schooner Hesperus,
  That sailed the wintry sea;
And the skipper had taken his little daughter,
  To bear him company.

Blue were her eyes as the fairy-flax,
  Her cheeks like the dawn of day,
And her bosom white as the hawthorn buds,
  That ope in the month of May.
💡

Longfellow uses simile extensively in this poem: “eyes as the fairy-flax,” “cheeks like the dawn of day.” Notice how these comparisons create a vivid picture quickly. The poem’s tragedy comes partly from how beautiful and innocent the girl is made to seem before the storm strikes.

Longfellow’s poem is about a wreck caused by a captain who ignored a warning. How does this contrast with Nat’s approach to the sea? What does Nat represent that the Hesperus’s captain did not?

Relative Clauses: Identify
🎬
Coming soon
“The Astrea, which was the fastest ship in Salem Harbor, reached the distressed vessel before nightfall.” — This relative clause is: Restrictive — it identifies which Astrea Non-restrictive — it adds extra info; the Astrea is already identified by name
“Any navigator who could calculate lunar distances was worth his weight in gold.” — This clause is: Restrictive — it defines which type of navigator (no comma, uses “who”) Non-restrictive — it adds optional extra info about the navigator
✏️ Show What You Know · Grammar
Expand each simple sentence by adding a relative clause. Then identify whether your clause is restrictive (no comma, uses “that”) or non-restrictive (comma, uses “which” or “who”).
a. “The Astrea rescued the ship.” + add a relative clause about the Astrea or the ship.
b. “Nat calculated the position.” + add a relative clause about Nat or the position.

Today’s focus: Today you check whether each section of your essay does its job. Strong organization means every paragraph has one clear focus and contributes to the claim.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex reads each section and writes one sentence saying what it does.

BEFORE
Alex’s draft diagnosis: Introduction does: sets up the claim. Body 1 does: shows Nat’s self-education. Body 2 does: um, talks about navigation stuff. Conclusion does: says the claim again.
AFTER
Alex’s honest diagnosis: Body 2 needs a clearer job. Alex refocuses it: Body 2 does: shows how Nat took his self-education and turned it outward — making it accessible to others, not keeping it to himself. Conclusion does: synthesizes Body 1 + 2 to show why the combination is what makes Bowditch extraordinary.

Notice: Alex’s honest assessment of Body 2 was “talks about navigation stuff” — too vague. Revising the job description forces Alex to clarify what the section actually argues. If you can’t say what a section does in one sentence, it probably needs reorganizing.

📋 Today’s Tool: Section Job Check

Write one sentence for each section of your essay: what does it do? Then check: does the section actually do that job?

Alex’s example (filled):

Introduction: Sets up the claim (fog scene → Nat’s background → thesis). ✓
Body 1: Shows Nat’s self-education (chandlery nights, notebooks). ✓
Body 2: Shows how Nat turned self-education outward — making knowledge accessible. Revised and refocused. ✓
Conclusion: Synthesizes both body sections and returns to the opening fog scene. ✓

Study the model, then fix the sentence

Sentence 1 — The Correct Model

Tap words you notice are correct. Watch for capitals, quotation marks, and correct word choices.

Hannah watched Kit and Nat talking and smiled because she knew they were becoming friends.

Sentence 2 — Fix It

This sentence has mistakes. Rewrite it correctly in your notebook, using the model as a guide.

nat eaton told kit that shakespeare should of gone on with the tempest story

Corrected:

Nat Eaton told Kit that Shakespeare should have gone on with The Tempest story.

Compare with the model: names are capitalized (Nat Eaton, Kit, Shakespeare), “should have” not “should of,” and The Tempest is a book title (italicized or underlined). The sentence needs a period at the end.

Morphology · Suffixes
Suffixes -ive and -ative
🌱
Teacher introduces -ive and -ative suffixes

This week you learned that the suffixes -ive and -ative turn verbs into adjectives. They mean “having the quality of” or “tending to.”

📌 Key Concept: -ive / -ative Examples

create → creative (having the quality of creating)

imagine → imaginative (tending to imagine)

act → active (tending to act)

perceive → perceptive (having the quality of perceiving)

🧩

Grammar · Explain
Why Dialogue Punctuation Matters
✏️
Teacher explains why dialogue punctuation matters

All week you have practiced punctuating dialogue. Good writers use quotation marks, commas, and new paragraphs to show who is speaking and keep conversations clear.

👉 From the novel

Well-punctuated: “Shakespeare should have gone on with the story,” Nat said. “He didn’t tell what happened when that young prince took her back to England.”

The quotation marks show exactly what Nat says. The comma before the dialogue tag keeps the sentence flowing. A new paragraph would start when Kit replies.

🎯

Practice: Pick the Correct Dialogue! Tap the sentence that punctuates dialogue correctly.

Nat said I'd like to take you back to the islands. “I’d like to take you back to the islands,” Nat said.
Hannah smiled. “Go with your friend, child,” she said softly. Hannah smiled, go with your friend child she said softly.
Kit asked do you believe in witches Judith. “Do you believe in witches, Judith?” Kit asked.

Spelling · Challenge
Schwa Challenge — Can You Find the Tricky Vowel?
🔠
Teacher introduces the spelling challenge

In unstressed syllables, vowels often reduce to a schwa sound — that lazy “uh” sound. The tricky part is that any vowel letter can make the schwa sound. Try each challenge below.

prim_r
Kit uses a primer to teach Prudence. Which vowel fills the blank — e or a?
primer (e makes the schwa sound in the second syllable — PRIM-er)
ref_ge
Hannah’s cottage is a refuge for Kit. Which vowel fills the blank — u or a?
refuge (u makes the schwa-like reduced vowel — REF-yooj)
cont_gious
Laughter can be contagious. Which vowel fills the blank — a or e?
contagious (a makes the reduced vowel sound in the second syllable — con-TAY-jus)
perc_ive
Nat can perceive Kit’s loneliness. Which vowel fills the blank — e or a?
perceive (e fills the blank — per-SEEV; remember “i before e except after c”)

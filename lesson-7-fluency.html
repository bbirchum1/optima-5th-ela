⚒️ Week 7 · Fluency

This week you read Chapters 5–8 of Johnny Tremain—from Johnny joining the printing shop and the Sons of Liberty, through the Boston Tea Party, the Port Act, and the Lytes fleeing Boston. This passage captures the moment everything changed: the night the tea went into the harbor. Read it aloud with full attention to pace, pause, and the feeling of that December night on Griffin’s Wharf.

This Week’s Fluency Passage — Chapter 6
He flew up Salt Lane in the opposite direction from the waterfront. Now they were flinging themselves down back alleys—faster and faster. They were running so fast it seemed more like a dream of flying than reality.

The day had started with rain and then there had been clouds, but as they reached Griffin’s Wharf the moon, full and white, broke free of the clouds. The three ships, the silent hundreds gathering upon the wharf, all were dipped in the pure white light. The crowds were becoming thousands, and there was not one there but guessed what was to be done—and all approved.
— Esther Forbes, Johnny Tremain, Chapter 6

🗺️ Your Fluency Path
1
Listen
Watch the video. Notice how the first sentences are short and quick—urgent, breathless. Then everything slows as the moon appears. Listen for how your teacher’s pace changes at “The three ships, the silent hundreds.” That slowdown is intentional. That moment is worth savoring.
2
Read for Accuracy
Read slowly, saying every word correctly. Watch for: flung, waterfront, gathering, Griffin’s, and the long final sentence. If you stumble, back up to the beginning of that sentence and try again.
3
Read for Smoothness
Read again without stopping. Pause only at the dash and at the period before “The day had started.” The long final sentence should move like a tide coming in—steady and unstoppable. Don’t rush it; let each phrase land.
4
Read for Expression
Read with feeling. The first sentence is urgent—they are running. Then the moon breaks free and everything changes. Let your voice become quieter and fuller at “The three ships, the silent hundreds.” This is a historic moment. Forbes slows down so you can feel it. So should you.
5
Record and Submit
When you feel ready, record yourself reading the passage with accuracy, smoothness, and expression. Upload your recording to Canvas.
✅ Fluency Self-Check
🎯Did I say every word correctly, including Griffin’s Wharf and waterfront?
🌊Did I let the short sentences be quick and the long final sentence move slowly and fully?
🎭Did my voice change tone when the moon broke free—going from urgent to still and solemn?
🌕Did the final sentence feel like it belonged to everyone there that night?

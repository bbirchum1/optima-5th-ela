How does Fleischman make a Gold Rush adventure funny — and why does that matter?

Sentence 1 — The Correct Model

“The lad here,” Captain Swain said with a twinkling glance, “has found me out.”

Sentence 2 — Fix It

I confess chuckled captain swain. The lad here has found me out then he pointed to the sea raven. Look at her gentlemen. Following us like a chick after a hen

Corrected: “I confess,” chuckled Captain Swain. “The lad here has found me out.” Then he pointed to the Sea Raven. “Look at her, gentlemen. Following us like a chick after a hen!”

Key fixes: quotes around all dialogue; comma inside “confess,”; capitalize Captain Swain; new speech = new quotes; capitalize Sea Raven (proper noun); new paragraph for the action beat; exclamation mark inside closing quote.

How Fleischman Makes You Laugh
Analyze three comic techniques from Chapters 4–6
🎭 Three Comic Techniques in BTGHS

1. Comic contrast — Putting things that should never touch right next to each other. A butler shoveling coal. A pig hiding in the captain’s cabin. Eighteen barrels of “spoiled” potatoes saving a vineyard. The comedy comes from the mismatch.

2. The straight face — Praiseworthy maintains perfect dignity in every absurd situation. He is funnier because he refuses to acknowledge anything is unusual. “As unconcerned as you please.”

3. The ticking clock — Comedy needs stakes. The pig must survive until Rio. The ship must make it around the Horn. The grapes must not die before Callao. The more urgent the situation, the funnier the absurd solution.

Find one example of comic contrast from Chapters 4–6 not mentioned above. Describe the mismatch and explain why it works as comedy.

💡

This week you draft your adventure scene. You have a plan (hero, problem, setting, stakes). Now you write the actual scene — a moment of action or decision, told with dialogue, reaction, and specific detail. Fleischman’s secret: make the reader hear the voice. Give your hero a way of talking that is all their own.

👀 Watch How — Alex’s Draft Scene

Alex is writing the scene where Maya is discovered on the research ship. Here is Alex’s rough draft:

“You,” said Dr. Fenn, holding a flashlight at her face. “You’re not crew.”
Maya didn’t move. She had planned for this moment — seventeen different versions of it — and every single plan had evaporated the instant the light hit her eyes.
“I know about the formation,” she said. “My mother’s data. I can prove it’s real.”
Dr. Fenn stared at her for what felt like forever. Then, slowly, he lowered the flashlight.
“You’re Gwen’s kid.”

Notice: dialogue that sounds like real speech, not a summary. A pause that shows tension. Specific detail (seventeen plans). The voice is Maya’s — nervous, over-prepared, suddenly blank.

Write your draft scene. Start in the middle of the action. Use dialogue. Show your hero’s voice.

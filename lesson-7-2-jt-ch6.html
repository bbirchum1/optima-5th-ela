Morphology · Practice
Matching dict & scrib/script Words
🌱
Teacher reviews dict and scrib/script roots with chapter examples

Two Latin roots appear throughout academic and literary vocabulary. Dict means “say” or “declare.” Scrib and script mean “write.” Knowing these roots helps you decode unfamiliar words.

📌 Con-/Com- in Chapter 6

You learned that con-/com- means “together” or “with.” Now identify these roots in the story of the Tea Party.

The colonists gathered to conspire (con + spire: breathe together/plan together)

Sam Adams called the meeting to convene (con + vene: come together)

The men acted in concert (con + cert: decided/agreed together)

Their plan was composed of many small acts (com + pose: put together)

From Chapter 6: Rab and Johnny composed their courage together, their plan quietly conceived to act in concert with the other Sons of Liberty who convened at Griffin’s Wharf that December night.

From Chapter 6: The Sons of Liberty acted in concert—their plan composed together in the attic above the printing shop, carried out by men who had convened for one purpose.

🌱

Practice: Prefix/Root Match! Tap each word, then tap its meaning.

conspire
to breathe together—to plot or plan secretly
convene
to come together for a shared meeting or purpose
concert
working or acting in agreement together
compose
to put together into an organized whole
combine
to join or merge two or more things into one
🌱 All matched! Con-/com- connects!

Grammar · Introduction
Appositives and Commas
✏️
Teacher models how to find appositives in sentences

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase placed next to another noun to rename or explain it. Appositives are set off by commas. They add detail without needing a separate sentence.

👉 From the novel

“Johnny, the most skilled apprentice, took charge of the order.” — The appositive “the most skilled apprentice” renames Johnny.

“Mr. Hancock, a wealthy Boston merchant, commissioned a sugar basin.” — The appositive “a wealthy Boston merchant” explains who Mr. Hancock is.

Test it: Remove the phrase between the commas. If the sentence still makes sense, you found the appositive.

🎯

Practice: Grammar Spotter! Each sentence contains an appositive. Tap the appositive phrase—the words between the commas that rename a noun.

Mr. Lapham, the elderly master silversmith, warned Johnny about his pride.
Dove, the clumsiest apprentice, fetched the cracked crucible from the shelf.
Paul Revere, the finest craftsman in Boston, offered to buy Johnny’s time.
The sugar basin, Mr. Hancock’s special order, had to be finished by Monday.
Mrs. Lapham, a practical and determined woman, insisted the work continue on the Sabbath.

Argument Writing — Day 2: Body Paragraph 1
✓ What Is a Claim? → ★ Reasons → ○ Evidence → ○ Draft → ○ Revise → ○ Publish

🎯 Today’s focus: Learn what REASONS are—the “because” statements that support your claim.

👀 Watch How — Alex

Yesterday Alex wrote: “Johnny’s pride in his talent is actually his biggest weakness.” Today Alex needs REASONS—answers to “Why do you think that?”

Claim

“Johnny’s pride is his biggest weakness.”

Reason 1

Because his pride makes him treat the other apprentices badly, which turns them against him.

Reason 2

Because his pride makes him ignore warnings, like when Mr. Lapham told him to be humble.

Notice: Each reason answers WHY the claim is true. Reasons aren’t proof yet—they’re the logic behind the argument.

📋 Today’s Tool: The Because Test

Take your claim. Add “because…” If what follows makes logical sense, you have a reason.

CLAIM + “because it makes him treat others badly” → ✓ Makes sense = REASON

CLAIM + “because he has brown hair” → ✗ Doesn’t connect = NOT a reason

Your claim: ___
BECAUSE ___ → Does this connect?

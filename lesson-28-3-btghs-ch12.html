Chapter 12 Question
What does a legend tell you about what a community values?

The miners of Hangtown have heard a story about a stranger who knocked a road agent nineteen feet uphill. They have given this stranger a name: Bullwhip. They have accepted him instantly. What does this tell us about what life in the diggings valued? And what does Praiseworthy do with this reputation?

📌 Transitions & Introductory Phrases

These words and phrases connect ideas in literary response writing. They tell the reader how one idea relates to the next.

However — contrast or limit an idea
Therefore — show a logical conclusion
Furthermore — add a related point
Meanwhile — show simultaneous events
Nevertheless — contrast despite what came before
Consequently — show a result or effect
In contrast — set two things against each other
As a result — show what followed from an action

Punctuation rule: When these begin a sentence or clause, they are followed by a comma: However, Praiseworthy remained calm. When they connect two independent clauses, use a semicolon before and a comma after: The ruffians held the stage; nevertheless, Praiseworthy did not flinch.

Read: Chapter 12 — “Bullwhip”
Campfires, Aunt Arabella’s picture, and a new name for a new man
📌 Reading Focus

Chapter 12 is a quiet chapter that carries enormous emotional weight. Walking home barefoot through the pine trees, Jack asks Praiseworthy about Aunt Arabella — and stumbles into the heart of what their whole journey is about. Then the town greets them with a legend. Praiseworthy, who values honesty and precision above almost everything else, decides to accept the exaggerated story. Watch for what this decision reveals about who he has become.

⏸️ Pause & Think 1

Jack and Praiseworthy walk home barefoot through campfire-lit trees. Jack says “Partners were the next best thing to being related.” What has changed between them over the course of the story?

📓
⏸️ Pause & Think 2

Praiseworthy tries three times to correct the miners’ exaggerated story. Then he gives up and plays along — going so far as to claim the distance was “twenty-three feet at least.” Why does he change his approach?

📓
📖 Reading Question 1
Jack wonders about Aunt Arabella’s picture in Praiseworthy’s carpetbag. Praiseworthy says he has “no right to it” — then Jack thinks he knows the truth. What is the truth Jack thinks he understands — and what does it reveal about Praiseworthy’s deeper motivation for this whole journey?
📓
📖 Reading Question 2
The miners of Hangtown give Praiseworthy the nickname “Bullwhip.” He tries to correct their story — then stops and accepts it, even adds to it. What does this change of approach reveal about how Praiseworthy has adapted to life in the gold fields?
📓
📖 Reading Question 3
At the end of Chapter 12, Jack and Praiseworthy have new names: Bullwhip and Jamoka Jack. Looking back over Chapters 10–12, explain how these three chapters together develop the theme of how character reveals itself under pressure. Use one specific moment from each chapter.

💡

This week: shift to literary response. You’ve been writing your own adventure story. Now you write about Fleischman’s. A literary response makes a claim about theme — what Fleischman is saying about courage, resourcefulness, loyalty, or hope — and supports it with specific evidence from the text.

📋 Literary Response Structure

Opening: Name the book, author, and your theme claim.
"In By the Great Horn Spoon!, Sid Fleischman suggests that true courage is not the absence of fear — it is acting well in spite of it."

Body: Two or three pieces of evidence from the text, each explained. Use transitions: However, Furthermore, As a result, In contrast.

Closing: Restate what Fleischman is teaching — and why it matters.

Draft your literary response. Make a clear theme claim and support it with evidence.

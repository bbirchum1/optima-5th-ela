Fixes: Capitalize Praiseworthy (proper name); add a comma after hat and after gloves to separate items in a series. Also note the prepositional phrase: “from the crook of his arm.”

Poetry Corner
A poem about loyalty, loss, and the end of a great voyage

Walt Whitman wrote this poem in 1865 after the death of President Abraham Lincoln — but the images of ships, captains, and voyages make it sing right alongside Jack and Praiseworthy’s journey. Read it slowly. Let the rhythm carry you.

📜 Week 25 Poem
“O Captain! My Captain!” (first stanza)
Walt Whitman, 1865 — public domain
O Captain! my Captain! our fearful trip is done,
The ship has weather’d every rack, the prize we sought is won,
The port is near, the bells I hear, the people all exulting,
While follow eyes the steady keel, the vessel grim and daring;
        But O heart! heart! heart!
          O the bleeding drops of red,
            Where on the deck my Captain lies,
              Fallen cold and dead.
💡

Whitman uses extended metaphor — the whole poem is built on one image: a ship that has completed a hard voyage, but the captain has died before reaching port. The “fearful trip” is the Civil War. The “prize” is the end of the war. Notice the contrast between the crowd’s joy (“the people all exulting”) and the captain’s stillness.

📌 Finding Prepositional Phrases

A prepositional phrase starts with a preposition and ends with its object. To find one, ask: where? when? how? in what way?

From Chapter 1: “in the creaking darkness / of her cargo hold” — “out of Boston harbor” — “with a bowler hat balanced on his knees”

Each phrase gives you a piece of the picture. Stack them and the setting comes alive.

📌 Stressed Syllables

In Cal•i•FOR•ni•a, the third syllable (FOR) is the loudest. In Sac•ra•MEN•to, the third syllable (MEN) carries the stress. Say each word aloud and feel which syllable you emphasize.

Read: Chapter 1 — “The Stowaways”
Two potato barrels, one plan, one wild bull of the seas
📌 Reading Focus

Chapter 1 introduces Fleischman’s voice: comic, fast, and full of surprises. Watch for how he builds humor through contrast — the elegant butler vs. the coal bunkers, the “wild bull of the seas” who turns out to have a heart. Pay attention to Jack’s one bold speech that changes everything.

📖 Read or Listen

Read Chapter 1 in your novel. If you have an audio version, listen while following along with the text.

⏸️ Pause: The Opening Scene

Fleischman opens with “It was not once upon a time.” What effect does this have? Why begin a comic adventure novel by insisting on the specific date?

📓
⏸️ Pause: “Nothing a Butler Cannot Do”

Captain Swain asks what a butler can do on a ship. Praiseworthy answers without blinking. What does his answer reveal about his character?

📓
📚
What Good Readers Do
Read the comedy beneath the surface

Fleischman’s humor works by putting two worlds that should never touch right next to each other. A butler — the most formal, rule-bound profession in Victorian society — ends up shoveling coal in a boiler room at the bottom of a Gold Rush ship. The comedy is the contrast. But something serious runs underneath: Jack and Praiseworthy choose each other over comfort, twice in the same chapter. That’s not comedy. That’s character.

📖 Reading Question 1
How do Jack and Praiseworthy end up as stowaways on the Lady Wilma? What happened to their money, and who is responsible?
📓
📖 Reading Question 2
Captain Swain offers Jack a job as ship’s boy — comfortable compared to the coal bunkers. But Jack refuses. Why? What does his refusal tell us about his character?
📓
📖 Reading Question 3
Praiseworthy says to the captain: “It is not the beginning of a race that counts, sir, but the end.” What does he mean? How does this quote apply to their situation in Chapter 1?

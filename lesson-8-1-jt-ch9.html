🌱 Morphology: Prefixes sub- and inter-
Define · sub- = under, below · inter- = between, among
🌱
Morphology · Introduction
Prefixes sub- and inter-
🌱
Teacher introduces the prefixes sub- and inter-

Two Latin roots appear in many English words. Dict means “say” or “declare.” Scrib and script mean “write.” Knowing these roots helps you unlock the meaning of unfamiliar words.

📌 Two Powerful Prefixes

The prefix sub- comes from Latin and means “under” or “below.”

The prefix inter- comes from Latin and means “between” or “among.”

submerge — to go completely under water

submarine — a vessel that travels below the sea

intercept — to stop something between sender and receiver

intervene — to come between two people or situations

From Chapter 9: Paul Revere’s network worked to intercept British plans. The agents learned to submerge themselves in ordinary life while gathering information.

🌱

Practice: Prefix/Root Match! Tap each word, then tap its meaning.

submarine
a vessel that travels below the sea (sub- + marine)
intercept
to stop something between sender and receiver (inter- + cept: take)
intervene
to come between two parties to change the outcome (inter- + vene: come)
interact
to act between people; to affect each other (inter- + act)

How to Figure Out Unfamiliar Words
🔤
Teacher models the 3-step vocabulary strategy with a word from this week’s reading

When you meet a word you don’t know, use these three steps in order:

📌 Let’s try it together: “artisan”
1
Read the Sentence
Find the word in the book. What clues does the sentence give you?
“Thirty artisans, mostly masters, from all over Boston were the center.”
Revere’s spy network has thirty people at its center. These men are described as “masters”—experienced people. The word artisan seems to describe skilled workers or tradespeople, not merchants or generals.
2
Look at the Word Parts
Do you see a root, prefix, or suffix you recognize?
art- + -isan
The root art- comes from Latin ars/artis, meaning “skill” or “craft.” The suffix -isan (from French) marks a person who practices a craft. So artisan = a person who practices a skilled craft. Think of Paul Revere himself—a master silversmith, an artisan.
3

Were we close?

✏️ Grammar: Possessive Nouns
Define · Apostrophe rules for ownership
✏️
Grammar · Define
Possessive Nouns
✏️
Teacher defines possessive nouns with Chapter 9 examples

A possessive noun shows that something belongs to someone or something. To form a possessive, you add an apostrophe.

👉 Three Rules for Possessives

Rule 1 — Singular noun: add ’s  →  Rab’s musket • Johnny’s hand • the shop’s forge

Rule 2 — Plural noun ending in -s: add ’ only  →  the soldiers’ boots • the horses’ saddles

Rule 3 — Plural noun NOT ending in -s: add ’s  →  the children’s voices • the men’s coats

🎯

Practice: Possessive Identifier! Tap the word that correctly forms the possessive.

He heard the soldiers’ boots on the cobblestones, not the soldiers’s boots.

Rab’s steady voice never showed fear, but Rabs’ did not either.

The men’s coats were dark against the November sky—not the mens’.

✏️

In your notebook, write three possessives: one for a character, one for a group of soldiers, and one using an irregular plural noun. Use characters from Johnny Tremain.

4
🔠 Spelling: Possessives vs. Plurals
Define · Apostrophe = ownership; no apostrophe = just more than one
🔠
Spelling · Define
Possessives vs. Plurals
🔠
Teacher explains possessives vs. plurals

A plural means more than one—no apostrophe needed. A possessive shows ownership—use an apostrophe. These look similar but mean very different things.

📌 The Key Difference

PLURAL (just more than one, no apostrophe): soldiers, horses, ships, artisans

POSSESSIVE (something belongs to it, add apostrophe): soldier’s boots, horse’s saddle, ship’s cargo

Watch out for the tricky middle case:

“soldiers” = a group of soldiers (plural)

“soldier’s orders” = one soldier’s orders (singular possessive)

“soldiers’ orders” = orders given to many soldiers (plural possessive)

🎮

Practice: Plural or Possessive Sort! Tap a word, then tap the column it belongs in.

Plural (no apostrophe)
Possessive (apostrophe)

✗ Just says what happens. No argument.

📌 The Three Plural Rules

Rule 1 — Add -s: Most nouns. ship → ships  |  soldier → soldiers  |  merchant → merchants

Rule 2 — Add -es: Nouns ending in -s, -sh, -ch, -x, or -z. church → churches  |  box → boxes  |  watch → watches

Rule 3 — Change y to ies: Nouns ending in consonant + y. colony → colonies  |  liberty → liberties  |  army → armies

Exception: Vowel + y: just add -s. (valley → valleys; monkey → monkeys)

🔠

merchant → merchants

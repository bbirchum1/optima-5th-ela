Morphology · Week 14
Word Families: navig- and inform-
🎬
Coming soon

This week reviews two key word families from the biography. A word family is a group of words that share the same root. Changing the suffix changes the part of speech — but the core meaning stays the same.

📌 Two word families from the biography

Family 1: navig- (Latin: to sail, to guide a ship)

navigate (v) — to find one’s way, especially by sea · navigation (n) — the science of finding position and charting a course · navigator (n) — one who navigates

Family 2: inform- (Latin: to give form or shape to; to pass knowledge to someone)

inform (v) — to tell; to give information to · information (n) — facts; knowledge communicated · informative (adj) — providing useful information

Grammar · Week 14
Title Formatting and Citations
🎬
Coming soon

When you publish your essay, you need to format titles correctly and write a source citation. Two rules:

📌 Title formatting rules

Rule 1: Italicize (or underline) book titles and ship names.

Latham, Jean Lee. Carry On, Mr. Bowditch. Houghton Mifflin, 1955.

Nat served as supercargo aboard the Henry on his first major voyage.

Rule 2: Use quotation marks around chapter titles and section headings.

The final chapter, “Man Against the Fog,” is the climax of the entire biography.

In “Sealing Is Safer,” Latham contrasts safe choices with meaningful ones.

Rule 3: MLA source citation for a book.

Author last name, First name. Title of Book. Publisher, Year.

Latham, Jean Lee. Carry On, Mr. Bowditch. Houghton Mifflin, 1955.

Which sentence uses title formatting correctly? The chapter Man Against the Fog ends the book. The chapter “Man Against the Fog” ends Carry On, Mr. Bowditch.
Which citation is correctly formatted? Jean Lee Latham, Carry On Mr. Bowditch, Houghton Mifflin 1955 Latham, Jean Lee. Carry On, Mr. Bowditch. Houghton Mifflin, 1955.

This week is cumulative review. You’ve studied four major word-analysis patterns during the CMB unit. Today you bring them all together.

🔍 Four patterns to remember

Schwa vowels (Week 11): Unstressed syllables often reduce to a schwa sound (/&schwa;/). You have to memorize these because you can’t rely on how they sound. Ex: navigation, astronomer, calculate

-ous / -ious / -eous (Week 12): All mean “full of” or “having the quality of.” The spelling depends on the root. Ex: hazardous, ambitious, courageous

Assimilated prefixes (Week 13): Prefixes change spelling to match the root’s first letter. Ex: sub + port = support; in + possible = impossible

Word families (Week 14): Changing a suffix changes the part of speech but not the core meaning. Ex: navigate → navigation → navigator

✏️

In your reading journal, write one example word for each of the four patterns above. Then write a sentence using one word from each pattern. You should have four words and four sentences total.

A choice between the safe path and the meaningful one

As you read, think about how this moment connects to the novel’s central theme: the choice between safety and meaning.

Today’s focus: A conclusion synthesizes — it finds the larger insight that connects your two body sections. Today you write a first draft using the 3-Move Formula.

👀 Watch How — Alex finishes the essay

Alex has written the introduction and two body sections. Now Alex needs a conclusion that synthesizes — not just repeats.

BEFORE
Alex's first attempt: In conclusion, Bowditch was extraordinary because he worked hard and helped sailors. These two things show he was a great person.
AFTER
Alex's synthesis draft: What Bowditch's story reveals, when you hold both body sections together, is not just that he was disciplined or that he was generous — but that discipline and generosity were the same impulse for him. He worked hard not to prove himself, but because he couldn't stand the idea of sailors dying from errors that mathematics could fix.

Notice: Alex's revised conclusion does NOT repeat the body sections. It finds the connection BETWEEN them — the idea that discipline and generosity were the same thing for Nat. That is synthesis.

📋 Today’s Tool: 3-Move Synthesis Formula

A strong conclusion has three moves: (1) Larger insight — what do your two body sections reveal together? (2) Return to hook — call back your opening image. (3) Why it matters — beyond Bowditch, what does this mean?

Alex’s example (filled):

Move 1 — Larger insight: What Body 1 + Body 2 reveal together → “discipline and generosity were the same impulse for him”
Move 2 — Return to hook: Call back the fog scene → “That December morning in Salem Harbor, when he navigated blind, was the proof.”
Move 3 — Why it matters: Beyond Bowditch → “He showed that knowledge’s highest purpose is not to be held, but to be given.”

Your turn — do the same in your reading journal:

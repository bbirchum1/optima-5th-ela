📌 Morphology Reflection: What Did You Learn This Year?

You studied 20+ roots and suffixes across six units this year. Each one unlocked a whole family of words:

aud/or (hear/speak) · rid/ris + lud/lus (laugh/play) · curr/curs + tract (run/pull)

fort + val/valid (strong/worth) · –ous / –ive / –ity (character/quality suffixes)

The most important skill: When you see an unfamiliar word, break it apart. Find the root. Find the prefix or suffix. Reconstruct the meaning. You now have the tools to do this for thousands of words — not just the ones you’ve studied.

🔬 Show What You Know · Morphology Reflection
Choose the one root family from this year that you found most useful — and explain your choice. Then: (a) name the root and its meaning, (b) list five words built from it, (c) explain in 2 sentences why these words matter for the reading and writing you’ll do in 6th grade and beyond.
One root family — five words built from it — two sentences on why it matters for your future.

📌 What Makes a Presentation Stick

Voice: Not just volume — it’s personality. Your presentation should sound like YOU speaking about something you care about, not a report being read aloud.

Tone: Match your tone to your content. A moment that was funny in the novel can be funny in your presentation. A moment that moved you can move your listener too — if your tone is genuine.

Pacing: Slow down at important moments. Speed up at exciting ones. Pause before a quotation. Don’t rush the ending.

The most important rule: Make eye contact (or talk to the camera). If your eyes are buried in your script, you’ve lost your audience.

✏️

In your journal: Choose one moment from the year’s reading that you think would be hard to present — because the emotion is complex, or the humor is subtle, or the language is beautiful. Write 2 sentences about how you would use voice, tone, and pacing to share that moment.

Your Year-End Portfolio: Three Parts
What goes in it — and how to make each part matter
📌 Portfolio Structure (3 Parts)

Part 1 — Reader’s Portrait: Who were you as a reader in September? Who are you now? What changed — and what caused the change? (3–4 sentences, specific and honest)

Part 2 — Text Spotlight: The text from this year that meant the most to you — a novel, a passage, a chapter. Introduce it, read a short excerpt aloud, and explain why it mattered. (2–3 sentences intro + excerpt + 2–3 sentences explanation)

Part 3 — Writer’s Statement: What did you discover about yourself as a writer this year? What is the one technique or skill that most changed your writing? How did you apply it in your adventure story, literary response, or comparison essay? (3–4 sentences)

📓 Reflection Question 1
Write your Reader’s Portrait — Part 1 of your portfolio. Who were you as a reader in September? Who are you now? Name at least two specific things that changed — and trace at least one back to a specific book, scene, or assignment from this year.
📓
📓 Reflection Question 2
Plan your Text Spotlight — Part 2. Which text mattered most this year? Name it, write 2–3 sentences about why you chose it, and identify the specific passage you will read aloud. Copy out the first line of your chosen excerpt here.
📓
📓 Reflection Question 3
Write your Writer’s Statement — Part 3. What did you discover about yourself as a writer? Name the one technique that most changed your writing, and show how you applied it in at least one piece from this year.

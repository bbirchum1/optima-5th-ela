🌱 Morphology: Suffixes -al and -ous
Define — adjective-forming suffixes

Morphology · Day 1
Suffixes -al and -ous: Define
🎬
Coming soon

This week’s suffixes are two of the most common adjective-forming endings in English. Both turn nouns into adjectives — they describe what something is like.

📌 The suffixes and what they mean

-al — means “relating to” or “of the nature of.” It turns a noun into an adjective.

naut (sailor/ship) + ical → nautical — relating to ships and sailors

math (measurement) + ematical → mathematical — relating to mathematics

practic + al → practical — relating to actual practice; useful

lun (moon, Latin) + ar → lunar — relating to the moon

-ous — means “full of” or “having the quality of.” It turns a noun into an adjective.

hazard + ous → hazardous — full of hazard; dangerous

error → erroneous — full of errors; mistaken (Latin: errōneus)

rigor + ous → rigorous — full of rigor; very demanding and thorough

courage + ous → courageous — full of courage

Words from the Age of Discovery

This week’s six vocabulary words all come from the world Nat is navigating — literally and figuratively. All six are adjectives. Notice: most of them use the suffixes -al or -ous from this week’s morphology lesson.

🔍 3-Step Decode: erroneous

Step 1 — Context: In Chapter 13, Nat discovers that Moore’s Navigator contains erroneous calculations that could lead ships off course — or onto reefs. The errors were small but dangerous.

Step 2 — Word Parts: Err- comes from Latin errare, meaning to wander or stray. -oneous makes it an adjective. To be erroneous is to have strayed from the truth.

Step 3 — Confirm: Erroneous means containing errors; mistaken; incorrect. An erroneous calculation in a navigation table is not just wrong — it could be deadly.

Define — what they add to a sentence
🎬
Grammar · Day 1
Prepositional Phrases: Define
🎬
Coming soon

Prepositional phrases are groups of words that begin with a preposition and end with a noun or pronoun (called the object of the preposition). They add detail about where, when, how, or with what.

📌 What prepositional phrases do

Common prepositions: at, by, in, on, of, for, with, through, across, between, beyond, during, after, before, beneath, toward, among, without, under, over, around

Without the phrase: Nat navigated.

With phrases added: Nat navigated by the stars through the fog with remarkable precision.

Notice how each prepositional phrase answers a question: by the stars (how?), through the fog (where/what condition?), with remarkable precision (in what manner?).

In expository writing, prepositional phrases add the specific detail that makes claims convincing. “Nat made discoveries” is vague. “Nat made discoveries aboard the Henry during his first voyage by checking the almanac tables against the actual positions of stars” is specific and convincing.

Which sentence uses a prepositional phrase to add useful detail? Nat found errors. He was very surprised. Nat found errors in every table throughout the almanac, and he was determined to fix them.
Find the prepositional phrase in: “During his first voyage, Nat corrected hundreds of errors.” During his first voyage — tells when Nat corrected the errors “hundreds of errors” — this is a noun phrase, not a prepositional phrase

🔤 Spelling: -ous, -ious, -eous
Discover — three spellings of one suffix
🎬
Spelling · Day 1
The -ous Family: Discover
🎬
Coming soon

This week’s spelling focus is the -ous, -ious, and -eous endings — three ways to spell the same adjective-forming suffix. They all mean “full of” or “having the quality of,” but knowing which spelling to use requires memorization.

🔍 Discover the three spellings

-ous (most common): hazardous, rigorous, enormous, glamorous

-ious (base word often ends in a vowel or soft sound): ambitious, harmonious, mysterious, glorious

-eous (base word often ends in -e or has a special vowel): erroneous, courageous, gorgeous, advantageous

Pattern tip: When the base word ends in -ge (like courage, gorge, advantage), keep the e before -ous → courageous. This preserves the soft /j/ sound of the g.

In your reading journal, sort these words into three columns: -ous / -ious / -eous:

Writer’s Workshop · Week 12 · DRAFT
Start Body Section 2 with a varied transition
✓ Research Claim✓ Plan★ Draft○ Add Craft○ Revise & Edit○ Publish
🎯

Today’s focus: Body 2 starts today. But first you need a transition that doesn’t just say “Another reason.” Today you learn to use transitions that show the logical relationship between body sections.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex starts Body 2 with a weak transition, then revises.

BEFORE
Alex’s first draft: Another reason Bowditch was extraordinary was that he corrected navigation errors.
AFTER
Alex’s revised opening: Furthermore, what made Bowditch’s self-education even more remarkable was how he put it to work — not to advance his own career, but to fix a problem that endangered every sailor at sea.

Notice: “Another reason” is a dead transition — it just adds without connecting. Alex’s revision shows the RELATIONSHIP: the self-education (Body 1) leads to and is connected to the correction of errors (Body 2).

📋 Today’s Tool: Transition Types for Body 2

Choose the transition type that best shows the relationship between your two body sections.

Alex’s example (filled):

Add + deepen: Furthermore, what made ___ even more remarkable was ___
Show the result: This self-education had a purpose: ___
Zoom out: Beyond his individual achievement, Bowditch also ___
Contrast: If self-education was how Bowditch built his knowledge, then ___ was how he used it

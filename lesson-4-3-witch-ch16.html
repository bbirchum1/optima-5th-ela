Sentence 2 — Fix It

This sentence has verb tense errors and a capitalization mistake. Rewrite it correctly in your notebook.

nat sit in the stocks yesterday and is grinning at the crowd with defiant humor

Corrected:

Nat sat in the stocks yesterday and grinned at the crowd with defiant humor.

Capitalize “Nat” (proper noun). Change “sit” to “sat” (past tense — “yesterday” tells us it already happened). Change “is grinning” to “grinned” (keep the tense consistent — both verbs should be past tense). Add a period.

Morphology · Practice
Using -able / -ible Words
🌱
Teacher reviews -able / -ible suffixes

The suffixes -able and -ible mean “capable of” or “worthy of.” They turn verbs into adjectives. Today, add the correct suffix to complete words connected to Chapter 16.

📌 Quick Reminder

bear (verb) + -able = bearable — able to be endured

remark (verb) + -able = remarkable — worthy of notice

sense (noun) + -ible = sensible — showing good sense

resist (verb) + -ible = irresistible — impossible to resist

🧩

Practice: Add -able or -ible to the root word in parentheses. Type the full word.

The punishment seemed (bear) to Nat, who grinned through his hours in the stocks.
Hannah’s kindness was (remark) — she welcomed everyone without judgment.
Kit knew that visiting Hannah was not (sense) , but she went anyway.
Nat found the idea of putting jack-o’-lanterns on William’s doorstep (resist) .
John’s decision to enlist in the militia was (respect) , even if it upset Dr. Bulkeley.
🌱 All correct! You built adjectives like a word scholar.

Grammar · Day 3
Fixing Verb Tense Shifts
✏️
Teacher models fixing tense shifts using Chapter 16

Good writers keep their verb tenses consistent. When you write about the past, stay in past tense. When you shift tenses without a reason, the reader gets confused.

👉 Tense shift vs. consistent tense

Inconsistent (wrong): Nat sat in the stocks and is grinning at the crowd. ← shifts from past to present

Consistent (correct): Nat sat in the stocks and grinned at the crowd. ← both verbs are past tense

🎯

Practice: Fix the Tense Shifts! Each sentence has a tense error. Tap the version with consistent verb tenses.

Which version keeps the tense consistent? Kit visited Nat in the stocks and tries not to cry. Kit visited Nat in the stocks and tried not to cry.
Which version keeps the tense consistent? Hannah looked at Kit and asked about William. Hannah looked at Kit and asks about William.
Which version keeps the tense consistent? John enlisted in the militia and is breaking with Dr. Bulkeley. John enlisted in the militia and broke with Dr. Bulkeley.

Spelling · Day 3
Applying Accent & Stress Patterns
🔠
Teacher reviews accent and syllable stress patterns

Every multi-syllable word has one syllable that gets more emphasis — the stressed syllable. Knowing which syllable is stressed helps you spell tricky words, because the unstressed syllables often have unclear vowel sounds.

📌 Quick Reminder

PUN-ish-ment — stress on the first syllable

re-PENT-ance — stress on the second syllable

con-se-QUENCE — stress on the third syllable

The stressed syllable is louder and clearer. Unstressed syllables are quieter — their vowels can sound mushy.

🎮

RWM — Read like a Reader, Read like a Writer, Mimic
“Thee has never escaped at all if love is not there.”
📖 Read like a Reader
Hannah is telling Kit that running away — from Barbados, from a loveless marriage, from anything — doesn’t count as freedom if love isn’t part of where you end up. What does this mean for Kit’s situation with William?
✏️ Read like a Writer
A theme statement is not one word (“love”) — it’s a full sentence that captures the author’s message. Hannah’s line is almost a theme statement itself. How could you turn it into one? Example: “True escape comes not from running away but from finding where love is.” Notice: no character names, no plot details — just a universal truth.
🖊️ Mimic
Write your own theme statement about escape, love, or sacrifice. Make it universal — no character names, no plot details. Just a truth that could apply to anyone.

What Good Readers Do
Track theme through consequences

Good readers notice when an author develops theme through consequences. In Chapter 16, multiple characters act on their beliefs — and each one faces a cost. Nat pranks William and gets banished. John enlists and breaks with his mentor. Kit keeps visiting Hannah despite the risk. Track what happens when characters stand up for what they believe.

🧠 As you read, ask yourself

What does each character sacrifice for what they believe in? Is the cost worth it?

How does Hannah’s wisdom — “thee has never escaped at all if love is not there” — connect to the choices other characters are making?

📕
Read: Chapter 16
Nat’s punishment, Hannah’s wisdom, John’s choice

Thanksgiving is cancelled by Governor Andros. Nat is caught placing jack-o’-lanterns in William’s windows — he’s arrested, put in the stocks, and banished from Wethersfield. Kit visits him. Hannah shares quiet wisdom about love and escape. Prudence is learning to write her name. John enlists in the militia, breaking with Dr. Bulkeley. Watch for what each character is willing to sacrifice — and what they gain.

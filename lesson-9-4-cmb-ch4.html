Proper Names — Where Do Capitals Go?

Teacher walks through today's sentences and explains the conventions

Step 1 — The Correct Model. Tap each highlighted word to see why it’s written the way it is.

Tap a highlighted word to see the explanation.
Step 2 — Fix It
at twelve years old, nathaniel signed the indenture that would bind him to ropes and hodges for nine years.

How many errors do you see? Rewrite this sentence correctly in your reading journal. Use the model above to guide you.

Corrected sentence: “At twelve years old, Nathaniel signed the indenture that would bind him to Ropes and Hodges for nine years.”

Four corrections: the sentence starts with At (capital for the first word), Nathaniel is a person’s name (proper noun — always capitalized), and Ropes and Hodges are both part of a business name (proper noun — both capitalized). The word indenture stays lowercase — it’s a type of document, not a proper name. Compare with the model: the same four words need capitals.

📜 Poem for the Week
There Is No Frigate Like a Book
by Emily Dickinson (c. 1873) — public domain
There is no Frigate like a Book
To take us Lands away,
Nor any Coursers like a Page
Of prancing Poetry —
This Traverse may the poorest take
Without oppress of Toll —
How frugal is the Chariot
That bears the Human Soul.

Why Writers Choose Active Voice
✏️
Teacher explains why active voice makes writing feel clear and direct
🔑 Why It Matters

Active voice puts the doer first. It’s faster, clearer, and more powerful — the reader sees who’s doing what, immediately. Passive voice makes readers work harder. Good writers use active voice most of the time, and save passive for when the doer doesn’t matter or isn’t known.

Today’s focus: After a week of reading and testing, it’s time to write your FINAL version of the claim. This goes at the end of your introduction. It should be the clearest, most specific sentence you’ve written all week.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex compares the Week 1 claim with the Week 1 Day 4 finalized version.

BEFORE
Week 1 Day 1 claim: What made Bowditch extraordinary was not just his genius, but his belief that navigation was learnable.
AFTER
Finalized claim after reading Chs. 1–4: What made Nathaniel Bowditch extraordinary was not his mathematical talent alone, but the fact that he treated every obstacle — poverty, indenture, self-education — as fuel, and directed everything he learned toward making navigation safer for sailors who depended on it.

Notice: Alex’s finalized claim is longer and more specific. Reading Chs. 1–4 gave Alex concrete details (poverty, indenture, self-education) that make the claim provable. Your claim should grow richer as you read.

📋 Today’s Tool: Claim Revision: Before and After

Write your Day 1 claim and your Day 4 version side by side. Note what changed and why.

Alex’s example (filled):

Day 1 claim
What made Bowditch extraordinary was not just his genius, but his belief that navigation was learnable.
Day 4 final claim
What made Bowditch extraordinary was not his talent alone, but the way he turned every obstacle into fuel and directed it all toward making navigation accessible.

What changed: Added specifics from reading (obstacles, indenture, fuel metaphor). Cut the vague word “just.”

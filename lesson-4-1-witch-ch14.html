What Good Readers Do
Set your reading lens

Good readers notice how a story’s theme builds through repeated conflicts. A theme isn’t stated outright — it develops each time a character faces a choice or clash that connects to the same big idea.

In Chapter 14, several conflicts surface: Kit’s longing for the sea, Nat’s bitterness about William, and the political storm over the colony’s freedom. Ask yourself:

🧠 Watch for this

What does Kit’s reaction to autumn and the Dolphin reveal about what she truly values?

Why is Nat angry about the diamond-paned windows? What bigger idea is at stake?

How does the news about Governor Andros connect to the theme of freedom?

Morphology · Introduction
Suffixes That Make Adjectives: -able and -ible
🌱
Teacher introduces the suffixes -able and -ible

The suffixes -ive and -ative turn verbs into adjectives. They mean “having the quality of” or “tending to.” When you add -able or -ible to a verb, you get an adjective that describes a quality.

📌 Word families

create (verb) → creative (adjective) — having the quality of creating

instruct (verb) → instructive (adjective) — tending to instruct

imagine (verb) → imaginative (adjective) — having the quality of imagining

act (verb) → active (adjective) — tending to act

Notice: Kit is creative and imaginative — both -ative adjectives that describe someone who creates and imagines. These suffixes show up constantly in descriptions of character.

Vocabulary · Introduction
How to Figure Out Unfamiliar Words
🔤
Teacher models the 3-step vocabulary strategy

Chapter 14 is rich with sensory words and emotional language as Kit experiences autumn and faces Nat’s bitterness. Use the three-step strategy: context, word parts, then confirm.

🔍 3-Step Decode: buoyancy

Step 1 — Context: “Every morning she woke with a new confidence and buoyancy she could not explain.” Something about autumn fills Kit with lightness — the word describes an uplifted, floating feeling.

Step 2 — Word Parts: Buoy (a floating object) + -ancy (a state of being). Together: the state of being buoyant — lifted up, light.

Step 3 — Confirm: Buoyancy means the quality of being cheerful and optimistic, or the ability to float. Kit’s spirits are lifted by autumn the way a buoy is lifted by water.

✏️ Grammar: Verb Tense Consistency
Learn — Define — what is verb tense consistency?
✏️
Grammar · Introduction
What Is Verb Tense Consistency?
✏️
Teacher introduces verb tense consistency

When you write a narrative, the verb tense must stay consistent. If you start in past tense, keep going in past tense. Speare writes the entire novel in past tense — “stood,” “held,” “burned,” “crackled.” She never accidentally jumps to present tense.

👉 Three rules for verb tense consistency

Rule 1 — Pick a tense and stay in it. Most narratives use past tense: “Kit stood in the doorway and held her breath.”

Rule 2 — Don’t shift mid-sentence. ✘ “Kit stood in the doorway and holds her breath.” The shift from past to present jars the reader.

Rule 3 — Dialogue can differ. Characters speak in present tense naturally: “I know where the charter went,” she said. The “said” stays in past tense; the spoken words are present because the character is speaking now.

Spelling · Introduction
Accent and Syllable Stress in Longer Words
🔠
Teacher introduces accent and syllable stress

Look at these two groups of words. Say each word out loud. Clap the syllables. Which syllable do you say LOUDER?

Group A — Stress on the FIRST syllable

REV-er-ence

EL-e-gant

CON-fi-dence

BEAU-ti-ful

Say these aloud. Which syllable sounds strongest — the first, middle, or last?

Group B — Stress on the SECOND syllable

re-LUC-tance

be-WIL-der-ing

mag-NIF-i-cent

Say these aloud. Which syllable sounds strongest here?

Syllable stress (or accent) means one syllable in a word is said louder, longer, and at a higher pitch than the others. In English, the stressed syllable often determines how we spell and pronounce the word.

Why it matters for spelling: Unstressed syllables often have reduced vowels that are hard to hear. Knowing where the stress falls helps you remember the correct spelling — REV-er-ence (not “reverance”) and EL-e-gant (not “elegent”).

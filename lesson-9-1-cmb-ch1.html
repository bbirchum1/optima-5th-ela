Ships, sailors, and carrying
🌱
Morphology · Introduction
Word Roots: naut / nav and port
🌱
Teacher introduces the roots naut, nav, and port

Nat Bowditch’s whole world is built around ships, the sea, and the work of carrying goods across the ocean. Two powerful Latin and Greek roots run through almost every word he knows:

🧭 The roots and what they mean

naut / nav — from Latin and Greek words meaning ship or sailor. You’ll find these in words about ships, the sea, and navigating.

port — from the Latin word portare, meaning to carry. You’ll find this in words about moving things from one place to another.

navigator → nav (ship) + the person who guides it → one who guides a ship’s route

nautical → naut (sailor) + -ical (relating to) → relating to ships and the sea

transport → trans (across) + port (carry) → to carry something from one place to another

portable → port (carry) + -able (able to be) → able to be carried or moved easily

Vocabulary · Introduction
How to Figure Out Unfamiliar Words
🔤
Teacher models the 3-step vocabulary strategy

Carry On, Mr. Bowditch is full of maritime and historical vocabulary — words connected to ships, the sea, and life in 1780s Salem. When you hit an unfamiliar word this week, use this three-step strategy to figure it out before checking a dictionary.

🔍 3-Step Decode: privateer

Step 1 — Context: “Father grinned. ‘They’re drumming up a crew for another privateer!’” Father says they’re finding a crew — the people who work a ship. A privateer needs a crew, so it must be some kind of ship.

Step 2 — Word Parts: The base word is private, and the suffix -eer often names someone who does something (musket–eer, pioneer). So a “private-eer” is a ship that operates privately — not as part of the official navy.

Step 3 — Confirm: A privateer is a privately owned ship that the government authorizes to attack and capture enemy ships during wartime. The crew keeps some of what they capture.

✏️ Grammar: Active and Passive Voice
Learn — define what makes a sentence active or passive
✏️
Grammar · Introduction
Active and Passive Voice
✏️
Teacher introduces active and passive voice

Writers can express the same action two different ways. Understanding the difference will help you as both a reader and a writer.

📌 Active Voice vs. Passive Voice

Active Voice: The subject does the action. The sentence moves forward: who does what.

“Nat solved the problem.” — Nat (subject) is doing the solving.

“Granny shook her head.” — Granny (subject) is doing the shaking.

Passive Voice: The subject receives the action. A form of “to be” (was, were, is, are) + a past participle shows this.

“The problem was solved by Nat.” — The problem (subject) receives the solving.

“The coals were dumped on the kindling.” — The coals receive the action.

Quick check: Passive voice almost always uses was or were + a verb ending in -ed or -en. If you can add “by [someone]” at the end and it makes sense, it’s probably passive.

🔠 Spelling: Vowel Teams in Maritime Words
Discover — two ways to spell the long /ā/ sound
🔠
Spelling · Introduction
Vowel Teams and the Long A Sound
🔠
Teacher introduces vowel team patterns in maritime vocabulary

Look at these two groups of words. Both groups make the same vowel sound — the long /ā/ sound as in say. But the spelling is different. What do you notice?

Group A

sail • main • captain • braid • strain

Look at where the long /ā/ sound appears. How is it spelled in each word?

Group B

gale • brave • navigate • wave • fate

Now look at this group. How is the long /ā/ sound spelled here?

💡

In Group A, the long /ā/ sound is spelled with ai — two vowels side by side. In Group B, it’s spelled with a–e — a vowel, then a consonant, then a silent e at the end.

The long /ā/ sound has two common spellings in maritime vocabulary:

ai (vowel team) — two vowels together: sail, main, cain

a_e (vowel-consonant-e) — vowel + consonant + silent e: gale, brave, navigate

Both patterns are extremely common in words related to ships, the sea, and Nat’s world. Knowing both helps you read and spell them confidently.

Writer’s Workshop · Week 9 · RESEARCH CLAIM
Write a research claim
★ Research Claim○ Plan○ Draft○ Add Craft○ Revise & Edit○ Publish
🎯

Today’s focus: A research claim is one sentence that answers the research question. It’s your whole essay in miniature — specific, arguable, and provable.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex is writing a research essay about Nat Bowditch. Alex reads Chapter 1 and writes a first attempt at a claim.

BEFORE
Nat Bowditch was really good at math and helped a lot of sailors.
AFTER
What made Nathaniel Bowditch extraordinary was not just his mathematical genius, but his belief that complex navigation could be learned by any ordinary sailor willing to try.

Notice: Alex’s “before” is a fact, not a claim. It’s vague (“really good”) and doesn’t say anything arguable. The “after” makes a real argument — someone could disagree and need evidence to be convinced.

📋 Today’s Tool: Claim Strength Test

A strong research claim passes three tests. Check yours against each one.

Alex’s example (filled):

Specific?
Names the quality or achievement, not just “Nat was great.”
✓ Alex names the specific belief (that knowledge belongs to everyone).
Arguable?
Someone could disagree and need evidence.
✓ You could argue Nat was extraordinary for other reasons.
Provable?
The book contains actual events that support it.
✓ Chapters 5–24 all show Nat teaching sailors and simplifying navigation.

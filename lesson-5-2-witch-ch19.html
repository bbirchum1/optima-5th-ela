Today, watch how a crisis reveals who people truly are. Kit stands trial for witchcraft, facing absurd accusations and a terrified crowd. Pay attention to who shows courage when it matters most—and who stays silent. Notice how one child’s voice can undo what fear has built.

Daily Oral Language
Study the model, then fix the sentence

Sentence 1 — The Correct Model

Tap words you notice are correct. Watch for capitals, commas, and punctuation.

Before the crowd, Prudence opened the Bible and read in a clear, steady voice.

Sentence 2 — Fix It

This sentence has mistakes. Rewrite it correctly in your notebook, using the model as a guide.

before the crowd prudence opened the bible and read in a clear steady voice

Corrected:

Before the crowd, Prudence opened the Bible and read in a clear, steady voice.

Compare with the model: “Before the crowd” is an introductory phrase and needs a comma after it. Capitalize “Prudence” (proper name) and “Bible” (proper noun). Add a comma between “clear” and “steady” (coordinate adjectives).

Sentence 3 — Fix It

suddenly the courtroom doors opened and nat strode in with prudence at his side her small hands clutching a worn copybook

Corrected:

Suddenly, the courtroom doors opened, and Nat strode in with Prudence at his side, her small hands clutching a worn copybook.

“Suddenly” is an introductory word and needs a comma after it. Capitalize “Nat” and “Prudence” (proper names). Add a comma before “and” joining two independent clauses. Add a comma before “her small hands” (appositive phrase).

Matching -ity and -ty Words
🌱
Teacher reviews -ity/-ty suffixes with chapter examples

The suffixes -ity and -ty come from Latin. They turn adjectives into nouns that mean “the state or quality of.” This week, match each word to its meaning using examples connected to Chapter 19.

📌 From Chapter 19

authority (author + -ity) — Captain Talcott held authority over the inquiry

reality (real + -ity) — Kit faced the reality that the crowd wanted her punished

cruelty (cruel + -ty) — The accusations showed the cruelty of mob fear

security (secure + -ity) — Kit longed for the security of knowing she would be freed

curiosity (curious + -ity) — Goodman Cruff stared at Prudence’s reading with curiosity

Grammar · Introduction
Commas After Introductory Phrases
✏️
Teacher models comma placement after introductory words and phrases

An introductory word or phrase comes before the main clause of a sentence. It sets the scene—telling when, where, how, or why—and is followed by a comma to signal that the main idea is coming.

👉 From the novel

Introductory word: “Suddenly, the courtroom doors burst open.” — “Suddenly” sets the scene.

Introductory phrase: “Before the crowd, Prudence opened the Bible.” — “Before the crowd” tells where.

Watch for: If the introductory part is very short (one word like “Then”), the comma is sometimes optional, but it is never wrong to include it.

🎯

Practice: Grammar Spotter! Each sentence is missing a comma after an introductory word or phrase. Tap the spot where the comma should go.

During the inquiry __ Captain Talcott called the witnesses forward.

During inquiry __ called

Trembling with fear __ Kit refused to reveal who owned the copybook.

Trembling fear __ reveal

Without warning __ Nat appeared in the doorway with Prudence beside him.

In a clear steady voice __ Prudence read the verse from the Bible.

In voice __ read

Finally __ Kit was declared free and innocent of all charges.

declared Finally __ innocent

Read: Chapter 19
The trial, Prudence’s courage, and Kit declared free

Kit is brought before the Town House for her witchcraft inquiry. Captain Talcott presides as magistrate. The accusations are absurd—cursing cattle, sticking pins—but the crowd believes them. Matthew protests, and Goodman Cruff produces Prudence’s copybook. Just as Kit is about to be sent to Hartford for trial, Nat appears with Prudence, who reads from the Bible. Goodman Cruff withdraws the charges, and Kit is declared free and innocent.

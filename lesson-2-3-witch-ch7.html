🌱 Morphology: Suffixes -ion / -ation
Turning verbs into nouns
🌱
Morphology · Practice
Using -ion / -ation Words
🌱
Teacher reviews -ion / -ation suffixes

The suffixes -ion and -ation turn verbs into nouns. When you add -ation, you change an action word into a thing or concept. Today, practice using these words in sentences connected to Chapter 7.

📌 Quick Reminder

admire (verb) + -ation = admiration (noun) — the feeling of admiring someone

submit (verb) + -ion = submission (noun) — the act of submitting

converse (verb) + -ation = conversation (noun) — the act of conversing

rebel (verb) + -ion = rebellion (noun) — the act of rebelling

🧩

Practice: Fill in the correct -ion / -ation word to complete each sentence about Chapter 7.

Grammar · Day 3
Combining Sentences
✏️
Teacher models sentence combining from Chapter 7

When two short sentences share a subject or describe the same moment, you can combine them into one stronger sentence using a compound subject or compound predicate.

👉 From the novel

Compound predicate: “William sat stiffly.” + “William stared at Kit.” → “William sat stiffly and stared at Kit.”

Compound subject: “Mercy smiled warmly.” + “Rachel smiled warmly.” → “Mercy and Rachel smiled warmly.”

🎯

Practice: Sentence Combiner! For each pair, tap the correct combined version.

“Kit popped corn. John popped corn.” Kit and John popped corn. Kit popped corn and John.
“Matthew questioned William. Matthew challenged William.” Matthew questioned and William challenged. Matthew questioned and challenged William.
“Kit knitted quietly. Kit watched William.” Kit knitted quietly and watched William. Kit and William knitted quietly.

Spelling · Day 3
Applying Suffix Rules
🔠
Teacher reviews suffix spelling changes

When you add a suffix that starts with a vowel, the spelling of the base word sometimes changes. Today, apply those rules to build new words.

📌 Quick Reminder

Drop the e: admire + -ation = admiration (drop the silent e before a vowel suffix)

Double the consonant: submit + -ion = submission (double the final consonant in a short-vowel word)

Y to i: envy + -ous = envious (change y to i before adding the suffix)

🎮

Practice: Sort these words by which rule was applied. Tap a word, then tap the column.

What Good Readers Do
Track how Kit’s feelings keep shifting

As you read today, notice how Kit’s perspective on William keeps shifting. She finds his silence frustrating, then flattering, then suffocating. Good readers track how a character’s feelings change — even within a single chapter.

🧠 As you read, ask yourself

When does Kit feel flattered by William — and when does she feel trapped?

What moments reveal that Kit’s perspective is more complicated than simply liking or disliking William?

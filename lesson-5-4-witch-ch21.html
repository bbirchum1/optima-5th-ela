🌱 Morphology: Suffixes -ity / -ty
Turning adjectives into nouns of quality or state
🌱
Morphology · Suffixes
Suffixes -ity and -ty
🌱
Teacher introduces -ity and -ty suffixes

This week you learned that the suffixes -ity and -ty turn adjectives into nouns that name a quality or state.

📌 Key Concept: -ity / -ty Examples

vital → vitality (the state of being full of life)

cruel → cruelty (the quality of being cruel)

pure → purity (the state of being pure)

prosperous → prosperity (the state of being prosperous)

🧩

Practice: Suffix Match! Tap a word, then tap its meaning.

Tap a word, then tap its meaning.
vitality
the quality of being cruel
cruelty
the state of being full of life
purity

prosperity

🌱 All matched! You know your -ity/-ty suffixes!
💡

Challenge: Think about -ity and -ty words you use every day. Kit found vitality in her new home. Nat showed loyalty by naming his ketch after her. How many -ity/-ty words can you spot in your own conversations or reading?

Grammar · Reflective Practice
Why Commas After Introductory Phrases Matter
✏️
Teacher explains commas with introductory phrases

All week you have practiced using commas after introductory words and phrases. A comma after an introductory element tells the reader, “The setup is done — here comes the main idea.”

👉 From the novel

With comma: In the spring meadow, Kit realized she did not want to leave.

Without comma: In the spring meadow Kit realized she did not want to leave.

The comma after “In the spring meadow” signals that the introductory phrase is finished and the main clause is beginning. Without it, the reader stumbles.

🎯

Practice: Pick the Correct Version! Tap the sentence that uses the introductory comma correctly.

After months of cold and hardship, Kit finally felt the warmth of spring. After months of cold and hardship Kit finally felt the warmth of spring.
Standing at the water’s edge she saw the ketch rounding the point. Standing at the water’s edge, she saw the ketch rounding the point.
With a smile on her face, Kit ran toward the wharf. With a smile on her face Kit ran toward the wharf.
🧠 Reflect

Why does a comma after an introductory phrase matter for the reader? Think about how Kit’s story would read if introductory phrases ran straight into main clauses with no pause.

🔠
Spelling · Suffix Challenge
Suffix Challenge — Decode the Word
🔠
Teacher introduces the suffix challenge

You have learned many suffix rules this unit — -ly, -ness, -ment, -able/-ible, -ity/-ty, and more. Today, solve each riddle to show you remember how suffixes change words. Try each challenge below.

I am the noun form of “cruel.” I end in -ty. What am I?
Think: the quality of being cruel.
cruelty — cruel + -ty = cruelty (the quality of being cruel).
I start with “radiant” but I name a quality, not describe it. I end in a suffix. What am I?
Think: the -ance or -ence suffix can turn adjectives into nouns too. But what about -ity?
radiance — radiant becomes radiance (not -ity this time!). Not every adjective uses -ity. This one uses -ance.
I describe something you can see through. Add -ity to make me a noun. Drop the silent “e” first!
Think: if glass is transparent, the quality is...
transparency — transparent → drop the -ent, add -ency. The suffix pattern shifts! (Also acceptable: pure → purity.)
I mean “full of life.” My adjective form is “vital.” Add -ity. What am I?
Kit’s Barbados strength gave her this quality.
vitality — vital + -ity = vitality (the state of being full of life and energy).

Read: Chapter 21
Spring arrives — double weddings, Kit’s realization, and Nat’s return

It is April. A double wedding is announced: John and Mercy, William and Judith. John has resumed his studies with Bulkeley — “he will think as he pleases.” William’s house is nearly finished. Kit secretly plans to sell her silk dresses and sail back to Barbados. But when spring arrives and Kit walks to the meadow, she realizes she does not want to leave. She understands at last: “It was not escape that she had dreamed about, it was love. And love was Nat.” In May, Nat arrives on a new ketch named The Witch — named after Kit.

Poetry Corner
A poem to notice and wonder about
O Captain! My Captain! (stanzas 1–2)
Walt Whitman, 1865
O Captain! my Captain! our fearful trip is done, The ship has weather’d every rack, the prize we sought is won, The port is near, the bells I hear, the people all exulting, While follow eyes the steady keel, the vessel grim and daring; But O heart! heart! heart! O the bleeding drops of red, Where on the deck my Captain lies, Fallen cold and dead. O Captain! my Captain! rise up and hear the bells; Rise up—for you the flag is flung—for you the bugle trills, For you bouquets and ribbon’d wreaths—for you the shores a-crowding, For you they call, the swaying mass, their eager faces turning; Here Captain! dear father! This arm beneath your head! It is some dream that on the deck, You’ve fallen cold and dead.
🧠 Think About This

Whitman wrote this poem after Abraham Lincoln was assassinated the day the Civil War ended—a leader who died just as victory was won. In Chapter 12, Rab dies at the battle of Lexington—right as the colonial cause wins its first real fight. What do both the poem and the novel say about the cost of victory? What does it mean when the person who fought for something doesn’t survive to see it?

Good thinking. Now let’s build the words you’ll need before we open the book.

Roots Across the Novel: Chapters 1–4

Teacher connects dict and scrib/script roots to words from across Chapters 1–4

Two Latin roots appear throughout academic and literary vocabulary. Dict means “say” or “declare.” Scrib and script mean “write.” Knowing these roots helps you decode unfamiliar words.

📌 Explain and Justify

You have now seen sub- and inter- across all four chapters of Week 8. Today you show you understand not just the meanings but the logic.

sub- words move downward or operate underneath: submerge, subterranean, subordinate, subside, subversive

inter- words work between or among: intercept, international, intermediate, intersect, intervene

From Chapter 12: Johnny stood on Lexington Green, feeling something submerged rise to the surface—grief, pride, a future. Rab’s sacrifice had intervened between his old self and whoever he would become next.

From Chapter 12: Johnny was no longer a boy who kept his subversive thoughts hidden. He stood on the Green, ready to step into an international story that had been building since that first morning in Mr. Lapham’s shop.

Possessive Nouns: Rules & Reasons
✏️
Teacher explains all three possessive rules with Chapter 12 examples

Today: explain possessive nouns in your own words. Then use what you know in writing.

📌 Explain What You Know

Rule 1 — Singular: add ’s  →  Rab’s, Johnny’s, Warren’s

Rule 2 — Plural ending in -s: add only ’  →  soldiers’, horses’, officers’

Rule 3 — Irregular plural: add ’s  →  men’s, women’s, children’s

From Chapter 12: “Rab’s musket, heavy and cold in Johnny’s hands, was the one gift the dying boy had to give.” Both are singular possessives.

🔠
Spelling · Explain
Putting the Rules Together
🔠
Teacher explains all three possessive categories

You have now sorted plurals, singular possessives, and plural possessives across the whole week. The apostrophe is the key—it means ownership, not just “something is missing.”

📌 The Complete Picture

No apostrophe = just more than one (plural): soldiers, rifles, horses

Apostrophe + s = one owner (singular possessive): Rab’s, Warren’s, Johnny’s

Plural ending in -s + apostrophe = many owners (plural possessive): soldiers’, officers’

Irregular plural + apostrophe + s = irregular plural possessive: men’s, children’s

From Chapter 12: “Rab’s musket, heavy and cold in Johnny’s hands”—two singular possessives in one unforgettable sentence.

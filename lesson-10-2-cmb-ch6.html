Daily Oral Language
Study the model; fix the tense error in Sentence 2

Sentence 1 — The Correct Model

Nat worked at his bookkeeping until the chandlery grew dark around him, and then he lit a candle and went on working.

Sentence 2 — Fix It

This sentence has a tense shift. Find it and rewrite the sentence correctly in your journal.

nat borrowed books from dr. prince and teaches himself latin at night by candlelight

Corrected: Nat borrowed books from Dr. Prince and taught himself Latin at night by candlelight.

Three fixes: “Nat” capitalized; “Dr. Prince” both words capitalized (title + name); “teaches” → “taught” (tense shift: “borrowed” is past, so “teaches” must become “taught”).

Poetry Corner
A poem about choosing effort when the easy path is gone

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow was a New England poet who grew up just one generation after Nat Bowditch. He understood something about what it means to keep rowing when there is no wind.

📜 This Week’s Poem
from “A Psalm of Life”
Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, 1838 — public domain
Lives of great men all remind us
  We can make our lives sublime,
And, departing, leave behind us
  Footprints on the sands of time;

Let us, then, be up and doing,
  With a heart for any fate;
Still achieving, still pursuing,
  Learn to labor and to wait.
💡

Sublime means noble or elevated. “Sands of time” is a metaphor for how quickly time passes and we are forgotten.

🤔 Think — Write in Your Journal

Longfellow writes “learn to labor and to wait.” Nat is nine years into an indenture he never chose. He works all day and studies all night. Where do you see him laboring? What might he be waiting for? How does this poem connect to “sail by ash breeze”?

Write your response in your reading journal. No digital textbox needed for poetry responses.

📌 Identify the root

biography → bio + graph (write) → a written account of someone’s life

chronometer → chrono + meter (measure) → an instrument that measures time precisely; sailors used it to find longitude

geography → geo + graph (write/record) → the written study of the earth

speedometer → speed + meter → measures how fast something moves

monogram → mono + gram (written mark) → one’s initials written as a single symbol

Today’s focus: Planning isn’t just deciding WHAT to say — it’s deciding WHICH evidence is strongest. Today you rank your Body 1 evidence.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex has found four possible pieces of evidence for Body 1 (the self-made scholar subtopic). Now Alex has to pick the two best.

BEFORE
All four options: (1) Nat studies at night in the chandlery. (2) Sam Smith teaches him navigation vocabulary. (3) Nat teaches himself Latin and algebra. (4) He fills notebooks with everything he learns.
AFTER
Alex’s ranking: (1) Chandlery nights — most powerful, shows volume of work. (2) Filling notebooks — shows method, not just effort. Cuts: Sam Smith — Sam helps, but the EFFORT is Nat’s. Latin/algebra — supports the first point, doesn’t add new subtopic.

Notice: Alex doesn’t use ALL the evidence. Using your best two pieces is stronger than listing four weak ones. The cut evidence isn’t wasted — it might appear in another body section or support your claim indirectly.

📋 Today’s Tool: Evidence Ranker

List 3–4 pieces of evidence for your Body 1 subtopic. Then rank them: which TWO are strongest?

Alex’s example (filled):

Alex’s subtopic: Nat as a self-made scholar

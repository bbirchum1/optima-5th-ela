Morphology · Introduction
Suffixes That Make Abstract Nouns: -ity and -ty
🌱
Teacher introduces the suffixes -ity and -ty

The suffixes -ity and -ty turn adjectives into abstract nouns — words that name a quality or condition you can’t touch. When you add -ity or -ty to an adjective, the word shifts from describing something to naming the idea itself.

📌 Word families

author (noun) → authority (noun) — the power or right to give orders

real (adjective) → reality (noun) — the state of being real

cruel (adjective) → cruelty (noun) — the quality of being cruel

secure (adjective) → security (noun) — the state of being secure

curious (adjective) → curiosity (noun) — the quality of being curious

Notice: Goodwife Cruff claims authority when she accuses Kit. The cruelty of the charge shocks Kit. The reality of a locked door strips away her security. These -ity/-ty nouns name the abstract forces at work in Chapter 18.

How to Figure Out Unfamiliar Words
🔤
Teacher models the 3-step vocabulary strategy

Chapter 18 shifts from warmth and gratitude to terror. Kit’s world changes in a single knock on the door. Use the three-step strategy: context, word parts, then confirm.

🔍 3-Step Decode: chagrined

Step 1 — Context: “Kit was chagrined to realize how much she had taken for granted.” Kit feels embarrassed and disappointed in herself — the word describes a humbling realization.

Step 2 — Word Parts: Chagrin (a French-origin root meaning distress or vexation) + -ed (past tense / adjective). Together: feeling distressed by embarrassment or humiliation.

Step 3 — Confirm: Chagrined means feeling embarrassed or annoyed because of failure or disappointment. Kit is chagrined that she didn’t appreciate the family’s care sooner.

✏️ Grammar: Commas with Introductory Words & Phrases
Learn — Define — when do introductory elements need a comma?
✏️
Grammar · Introduction
Commas After Introductory Words and Phrases
✏️
Teacher introduces commas with introductory elements

When a sentence begins with an introductory word, phrase, or clause before the main subject and verb, you set it off with a comma. This comma tells the reader: “Pause here — the main idea is coming next.”

👉 Three patterns for introductory commas

Pattern 1 — Introductory word: “Suddenly, the constable appeared at the door.” A single word like suddenly, however, meanwhile, finally gets a comma.

Pattern 2 — Introductory phrase: “After the long night, Kit leaned against the settle.” A prepositional or participial phrase at the start gets a comma.

Pattern 3 — Introductory clause: “When the door opened, Kit saw the constable standing behind it.” A dependent clause before the main clause gets a comma.

🔠
Spelling · Introduction
Cumulative Suffix Rule Review
🔠
Teacher reviews suffix spelling rules from Weeks 1–4

This week we pull together every suffix spelling rule you’ve learned. Look at these groups — each one follows a different rule. Can you spot the pattern?

Group A — Drop the silent e

create → creative

notice → noticeable

secure → security

What happens to the final e when the suffix starts with a vowel?

Group B — Change y to i

cruel → cruelty

curious → curiosity

beauty → beautiful

What happens when the base word ends in a consonant + y?

Rule 1 — Drop silent e before a suffix that starts with a vowel: create → creative, secure → security. (Keep the e before consonant suffixes: notice → noticeable keeps the e to preserve the soft c.)

Rule 2 — Change y to i when adding a suffix to a word ending in consonant + y: cruel → cruelty, curious → curiosity. (Exception: keep y before -ing.)

Rule 3 — Double the final consonant in one-syllable words with a short vowel + single consonant: stop → stopping, big → bigger.

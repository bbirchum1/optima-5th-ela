What Good Readers Do
Notice how an author handles a pivotal moment

Chapter 10 is a hinge in this book. After nine years, Nat’s indenture ends — and Latham must decide how to write this moment. Good readers notice how authors handle pivotal events: does the author dwell on emotion, rush past it, or show it obliquely? As you read, pay close attention to the craft choices Latham makes.

🧠 Watch for this

How does Latham write the moment of Nat’s freedom? Does she dwell on Nat’s feelings, or show them through action?

Fixes: capitalize “The”; “Ropes and Hodges” (proper noun); subject is “years” (plural) → “have” not “has.”

Poetry Corner
A poem about the call of the sea — on the day Nat finally answers it

Today Nat’s indenture ends and he boards his first ship. John Masefield wrote this poem in 1902 — but it speaks to something sailors have always felt. Read it today, the day Nat finally goes to sea.

📜 Poetry Corner
“Sea Fever”
John Masefield, 1902 — public domain
I must go down to the seas again, to the lonely sea and the sky,
And all I ask is a tall ship and a star to steer her by;
And the wheel’s kick and the wind’s song and the white sail’s shaking,
And a grey mist on the sea’s face, and a grey dawn breaking.

I must go down to the seas again, for the call of the running tide
Is a wild call and a clear call that may not be denied.
💡

Notice: Masefield uses repetition (“I must go down to the seas again”) to make the calling feel inescapable. Also notice: the speaker uses “and” many times in a row — that technique is called polysyndeton, and it creates a rushing, building effect.

📌 New words to identify

geophysics → geo + physics → the physics of the earth: gravity, magnetism, seismic activity

subterranean → sub (below) + terr + anean → below the earth’s surface

Mediterranean → medi (middle) + terr + anean → the “middle of the land” sea

geocentric → geo + centric (center) → having the earth as the center (old model of the solar system)

terrace → terr + ace → a flat area of land; also a flat platform of a building

Grammar · Day 2
Identifying Agreement Errors
🎬
Coming soon

Yesterday you defined the rule. Today, identify errors in sentences about Nat’s world — then show what you know.

“The tables in Moore’s Almanac was full of errors.” Error — “tables” is the subject (plural); should be “were” Correct as written
“The ship, along with its crew of twelve, was bound for Cadiz.” Error — “along with its crew” makes the subject plural Correct — “ship” is singular; “along with” is a phrase, not “and”
✏️ Show What You Know · Grammar

Today’s focus: Your hook is written. Now connect it to your claim using a bridge sentence — then end with your thesis. Today you write the full introduction.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex assembles hook + bridge + claim into a 4-sentence introduction.

BEFORE
Alex’s three separate parts sitting next to each other with no connection.
AFTER
Alex’s complete introduction: “A ship navigated blind into Salem Harbor in December fog — no landmarks, no visibility — guided only by one man’s calculations. That man was Nathaniel Bowditch, a self-taught mathematician from Salem, Massachusetts, who had spent his entire life turning complex navigation into something any sailor could learn. He had not grown up wealthy or privileged; he had been indentured at twelve and educated himself at night for nine years. What made him extraordinary was not his mathematical talent alone, but his relentless belief that the knowledge that protected sailors’ lives should belong to everyone.”

Notice: Alex’s introduction has four moves: (1) hook scene, (2) who is this man?, (3) quick context (indentured, self-taught), (4) claim. Each sentence does a specific job.

📋 Today’s Tool: Introduction Map: 4 Moves

Fill in the four moves for your introduction. Don’t worry about perfect wording — get the ideas down.

Alex’s example (filled):

Move 1: Hook
A ship navigated blind into Salem Harbor in December fog — guided only by one man’s calculations.
Move 2: Who is this person?
That man was Nathaniel Bowditch, a self-taught mathematician from Salem, Massachusetts.
Move 3: Quick context
He had been indentured at twelve and educated himself at night for nine years.
Move 4: Claim / Thesis
What made him extraordinary was his belief that navigation should be accessible to every sailor.

What Good Readers Do
Keep watching character — especially when the stakes go up

Yesterday you noticed what Nat wants and how he responds when things go wrong. Good readers keep watching that pattern as the story grows. In Chapter 2, something shifts: Nat is offered a chance to take a real risk.

When a character makes a surprising choice — especially one that costs something — good readers ask why. Hold these questions as you read:

🧠 Watch for this

What does spending his only coin tell you about what Nat values? What does it say about how he thinks about the future?

Poetry Corner
A poem about the voyage a book can take you on

Emily Dickinson wrote this poem in the 1870s — just a few decades after Nathaniel Bowditch’s time. She had never been to sea, but she understood something important about what reading can do.

📜 Poem for the Week
There Is No Frigate Like a Book
by Emily Dickinson (c. 1873) — public domain
There is no Frigate like a Book
To take us Lands away,
Nor any Coursers like a Page
Of prancing Poetry —
This Traverse may the poorest take
Without oppress of Toll —
How frugal is the Chariot
That bears the Human Soul.
💡

A few unfamiliar words: A frigate is a large, fast sailing ship. Coursers are fast horses. A traverse is a journey across something. A toll is money paid to travel a road or cross a bridge.

🤔 Think About This

Dickinson says a book is like a frigate — a ship that can take us to distant lands. Nat Bowditch lives surrounded by real ships and a real harbor. Why might this poem feel especially true as you begin reading his story? Where is the book carrying you?

Write your first reaction. What does Dickinson compare a book to, and why does that comparison fit the story you’re reading? This journal response is just for you.

🌱 Morphology: Roots naut / nav and port — Review & Extend
Same roots, new words — deeper understanding
🌱
Morphology · Review
Extending naut / nav and port
🌱
Teacher reviews the roots and introduces new examples

You already know that naut/nav means ship or sailor, and port means carry. Now see how far these roots travel — even beyond the sea.

🧩 New words, same roots

aeronautics → aero (air) + naut (ship/travel) → the study and science of air travel. Early hot-air balloonists were called “aeronauts” — sailors of the air.

navy → nav (ship) → a country’s official fleet of warships. Nat reads about real naval battles throughout this story.

export → ex (out) + port (carry) → to carry goods out of a country for sale elsewhere. Salem merchants export and import goods from across the world.

report → re (back) + port (carry) → to carry information back; an account of events. Nat will write detailed navigational reports during his voyages.

✏️
Grammar · Practice
Identifying Active and Passive Voice
✏️
Teacher models how to spot active and passive voice

Yesterday you learned the difference between active voice (the subject does the action) and passive voice (the subject receives the action). Today, identify which is which in sentences from Nat’s story.

📌 Quick reminder

Active: Subject + action verb. “Father led Nat to the docks.” (Father does the leading.)

Passive: was/were + past participle. “Nat was led to the docks by Father.” (Nat receives the action.) Look for was or were as your signal.

🎯

🔠 Spelling: Long /ā/ — Identify the Pattern in New Words
Identify — ai or a_e? Put your pattern knowledge to work
🔠
Spelling · Practice
Long /ā/ in New Words
🔠
Teacher introduces new words for the ai and a_e sort

You know two spellings for the long /ā/ sound: ai (two vowels together, like sail) and a_e (vowel + consonant + silent e, like brave). Now identify the pattern in new words — all from Nat’s world.

💡

Your clue: listen for where the long /ā/ sound sits in the word. If it’s in the middle followed by more consonants, it’s likely ai. If the long /ā/ leads to the end of the word with a silent e after a consonant, it’s likely a_e.

Writer’s Workshop · Week 9 · RESEARCH CLAIM
Test and strengthen your claim
★ Research Claim○ Plan○ Draft○ Add Craft○ Revise & Edit○ Publish
🎯

Today’s focus: The best claims can survive a counterargument. Today you test your claim by playing devil’s advocate — arguing against yourself.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex tests the claim by imagining what a skeptic would say.

BEFORE
Someone could argue: Bowditch was just lucky to have access to books and time to study.
AFTER
Alex’s response: That’s exactly why the claim is interesting — he DIDN’T have those advantages. He was indentured, self-taught, and worked at night. That makes the claim stronger, not weaker.

Notice: Testing your claim against the strongest counterargument doesn’t weaken your essay — it makes your evidence selection stronger. If the counterargument is too easy to defeat, your claim is too obvious.

📋 Today’s Tool: Claim Test: Devil's Advocate

Write the strongest possible argument AGAINST your claim. Then explain why your evidence still wins.

Alex’s example (filled):

Alex’s claim: What made Bowditch extraordinary was his belief that complex navigation was accessible to any willing sailor.

Strongest counterargument: He was just lucky — right place, right time, access to books.

Alex’s response: Except he had NO luck — indentured at 12, no school, taught himself at night. That’s not luck; that’s character.

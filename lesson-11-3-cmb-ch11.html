A ___ globe shows the continents and oceans of the earth.

A navigator who knows the ___ of a coastline — its hills, harbors, and shoals — can sail it more safely.
Correct! Terrain (terr = land) refers to the physical features of an area.
Nat’s study of ___ — the earth’s shapes and sizes — gave him tools for calculating position and distance.
Correct! Geometry (geo + meter = measuring the earth) gave Nat key navigation tools.

Grammar · Day 3
Using Correct S-V Agreement
🎬
Coming soon

This paragraph about Nat at sea has multiple subject-verb agreement errors. Identify the subject of each underlined verb, then rewrite the whole paragraph correctly in your reading journal.

Nat, along with the sailors, spend his evenings calculating position. The measurements of the stars requires a precise instrument. His notebooks, filled with corrections, sits on the table. The captain, as well as the crew, trust Nat’s mathematical skill completely.

Corrected: Nat, along with the sailors, spends his evenings calculating position. The measurements of the stars require a precise instrument. His notebooks, filled with corrections, sit on the table. The captain, as well as the crew, trusts Nat’s mathematical skill completely.

Key: “along with” / “as well as” = phrases that do NOT make the subject plural. Subject remains the singular noun before the phrase.

Write your corrected paragraph in your reading journal.

What Good Readers Do
Explain how events develop plot and character

At sea for the first time, Nat has to put everything he learned in the chandlery — mathematics, navigation, astronomy — into actual use. Good readers notice when a character has a moment of integration: when everything they’ve been building finally comes together into something real.

Writer’s Workshop · Week 11 · DRAFT
Draft your first body section
✓ Research Claim✓ Plan★ Draft○ Add Craft○ Revise & Edit○ Publish
🎯

Today’s focus: Your introduction is written. Now the first body section. Every body section has the same structure: topic sentence → Fact → Explanation → Significance. Today you build it piece by piece.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex builds Body 1 sentence by sentence using the F+E+S structure.

BEFORE
Alex’s attempt without structure: Nat studied a lot in the chandlery. He filled many notebooks. This shows he was dedicated.
AFTER
Alex’s F+E+S version: Topic: One quality that made Bowditch extraordinary was his commitment to self-education despite having no time, money, or formal schooling. Fact: For nine years at Ropes and Hodges, he stayed up after the chandlery closed, filling notebook after notebook with everything he could learn — navigation, Latin, algebra, astronomy. Explanation: This kind of sustained effort, night after night for nearly a decade, is not ordinary intelligence — it is disciplined character. Significance: It proves the claim because his knowledge came not from privilege but from choice, which is exactly what made it possible for him to later teach it to others.

Notice: Alex’s “before” has the right facts but no structure. The F+E+S version transforms three weak sentences into a complete argument. The significance sentence directly ties back to the claim.

📋 Today’s Tool: F+E+S Builder (Body 1)

Build your Body 1 piece by piece using the four-part structure.

Alex’s example (filled):

Topic Sentence
One quality that made Bowditch extraordinary was his commitment to self-education despite having no time, money, or formal schooling.
Fact (What happened?)
For nine years at Ropes and Hodges, he stayed up after the chandlery closed, filling notebook after notebook with navigation, Latin, algebra, and astronomy.
Explanation (What does this mean?)
This kind of sustained effort, night after night for a decade, is not ordinary intelligence — it is disciplined character.
Significance (Why does it prove the claim?)
His knowledge came not from privilege but from choice — which is exactly what made it later possible for him to teach it to others.

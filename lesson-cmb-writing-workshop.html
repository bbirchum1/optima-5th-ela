What is a research claim?

Your claim is the one-sentence answer to the research question. Every piece of evidence in your essay should support this claim. A strong claim is:

  • Specific — not “Nat was great” but what specifically made him extraordinary
  • Arguable — someone could disagree and need evidence to be convinced
  • Provable — you can back it up with specific events from the novel
Example: “What made Nathaniel Bowditch extraordinary was not just his mathematical genius but his determination to make that genius available to every ordinary sailor who needed it.”

Section 1 — Weeks 9–10
Introduction
Hook → context → thesis

What belongs in your introduction? (3–5 sentences)

  • Hook: Start with something vivid, surprising, or thought-provoking that pulls the reader in. Don’t start with “In this essay I will…”
  • Context: Briefly introduce Nathaniel Bowditch — who he was, when he lived, what he accomplished.
  • Thesis: End with your research claim, stated clearly.
Hook idea: Start with the fog scene from Chapter 24 — a ship navigating blind through impenetrable fog, the crew terrified, guided only by one man’s mathematics. Then zoom out: “That man was Nathaniel Bowditch…”

Body Section 1

What belongs in Body Section 1? (4–7 sentences)

  • Topic sentence: Name the quality or achievement you’re arguing supports your claim.
  • Evidence (Fact + Explanation + Significance):
    • Fact: What happened? (specific event from the novel, with chapter reference)
    • Explanation: What does this fact mean or show?
    • Significance: How does this support your claim?
  • Closing sentence: Connect back to your main claim.
Topic sentence example: “One quality that made Bowditch extraordinary was his refusal to accept errors that put sailors in danger.”

What belongs in Body Section 2? (4–7 sentences)

  • Varied transition + topic sentence: Don’t just say “Another reason” — use a transition that shows the relationship between your two body sections.
    • Add: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition to this…
    • Contrast: However, Beyond just X, what also distinguished him was…

Section 5 — Week 14
Conclusion
Synthesize — don’t just repeat
▾

What belongs in your conclusion? (3–5 sentences)

A strong conclusion synthesizes your argument — it shows how your points connect into a larger insight — rather than just repeating what you already said.

  • Synthesis (not repetition): What do your two or three body sections, taken together, reveal about Bowditch? What is the larger insight?
  • Return to the opening: Call back to your hook — if you started with the fog scene, return to it here with new meaning now that the reader knows everything you’ve argued.
  • Closing thought: Why does this matter beyond Bowditch himself? What can readers take from his story?
What NOT to write: “In conclusion, I have shown that Nat was extraordinary because he was good at math and also because he helped sailors.” (This just repeats.)

What to write instead: “Taken together, these examples reveal that Bowditch’s greatness was not the knowledge itself but his insistence that knowledge belongs to everyone — that no calculation is too complex for an ordinary person who is willing to learn.”

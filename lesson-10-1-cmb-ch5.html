Write, record, measure — the language of science and navigation

Morphology · Introduction
Word Roots: graph/gram and meter
🌱
Teacher introduces the roots graph/gram and meter

Nat Bowditch’s world is built on measurement and record-keeping. Two powerful Greek roots run through almost every scientific and navigational word he will ever need:

📌 The roots and what they mean

graph / gram — from the Greek word meaning to write or to record. You find these in words about writing things down, drawing things out, or sending recorded messages.

meter — from the Greek word meaning to measure. You find this in words about measuring things — temperature, distance, speed, altitude.

paragraph → para (beside) + graph (written mark) → a section of written text beside a mark

telegram → tele (far) + gram (written/recorded) → a message recorded and sent far away

diagram → dia (across/through) + gram (written) → a drawing that explains something by showing it across the page

thermometer → thermo (heat) + meter (measure) → an instrument that measures heat

barometer → baro (weight/pressure) + meter (measure) → an instrument that measures air pressure

Vocabulary · Introduction
Words That Open Nat’s World
🔤
Teacher introduces this week's vocabulary words

This week’s vocabulary comes from the world Nat is entering: the world of mathematics, navigation, and self-made scholarship. When you hit an unfamiliar word, use the 3-step strategy to figure it out.

🔍 3-Step Decode: apprentice

Step 1 — Context: The indenture says Nat has “put himself… as apprentice to Ropes and Hodges to learn the Art, Trade or Mystery of Ship Chandlery for a term of nine years.” Nat is agreeing to learn a trade from someone else for a long time.

Step 2 — Word Parts: The root comes from Old French apprendre meaning to learn. The suffix -ice names a person. An apprentice is a person who learns.

Step 3 — Confirm: An apprentice is someone legally bound to work for a master craftsman for a set number of years in exchange for learning that trade. Nat is bound to Ropes and Hodges for nine years to learn ship chandlery.

✏️ Grammar: Verb Tense in Informational Writing
Define — what is verb tense, and why does it matter?
✏️
Grammar · Introduction
Verb Tense: Past, Present, Future
✏️
Teacher introduces verb tense and why consistent tense matters

When you write informational text about a person like Nathaniel Bowditch, verb tense tells your reader when things happened. Choosing a tense — and sticking with it — is one of the most important skills in informational writing.

📌 The three main tenses

Past tense — describes what already happened. Most biographies use past tense. “Nat signed the indenture and walked home alone.”

Present tense — describes what is happening now, or what is always true. Often used in science writing. “A barometer measures air pressure.”

Future tense — describes what will happen. Usually formed with will or shall. “Nat will spend nine years at Ropes and Hodges.”

Tense shift: When a writer accidentally switches tenses within a passage — starting in past and suddenly jumping to present — it confuses the reader. Good informational writers maintain one consistent tense.

🔤 Spelling: Syllable Division in Technical Words
Discover — find the rule by looking at the pattern
🔤
Spelling · Discover
How to Break a Word Into Syllables
🔤
Teacher introduces syllable division with VCV and VCCV patterns

Nat is about to enter a world of long, technical words — barometer, logarithm, celestial, almanac. One key to spelling and reading these words confidently is knowing where to divide the syllables.

🔍 Two main division patterns

VCV pattern (Vowel-Consonant-Vowel) — When two vowels surround a single consonant, look at the first vowel. If it’s a long vowel sound, divide before the consonant: na·val, me·ter, ba·ro.

VCCV pattern (Vowel-Consonant-Consonant-Vowel) — When two consonants sit between vowels, divide between the two consonants: al·ma·nac, bar·rel, sig·nal.

The discover rule: Technical words follow the same patterns as everyday words. Sound it out, mark the vowels, then divide where the pattern says to divide.

🤔

Try it: Mark the vowels (v) and consonants (c) in these words, then decide where to divide each syllable. Write the syllable breaks in your journal using dots: ba·rom·e·ter.

✏️ Words to divide in your journal

al·ma·nac — al (closed: short a) + ma (open: long a) + nac (closed)

la·ti·tude — la (open: long a) + ti (open: long i) + tude

ce·les·tial — ce + les + tial

log·a·rithm — log (closed) + a + rithm

Morphology · Introduction
Morphemes — The Building Blocks
🌱
Teacher introduces this week's morphology focus

A morpheme is the smallest part of a word that carries meaning. This week we are breaking words into their pieces: prefixes, roots, and suffixes. When you recognize these pieces, unfamiliar words become puzzles you can solve.

📌 Examples from Chapter 3

recognition — re- (again) + cognit (know) + -ion (act of) = the act of knowing again

unwilling — un- (not) + will (desire) + -ing (showing) = not wanting to

uncomfortable — un- (not) + comfort (ease) + -able (capable of) = not at ease

disapproval — dis- (opposite) + approv (accept) + -al (act of) = the act of not accepting

🧩

Practice: Fill in the missing root or base word. The prefix and suffix are there to help you.

re- + + -ion = the act of knowing again
un- + + -ing = not wanting to
un- + + -able = not at ease
dis- + + -al = the act of not accepting
🌱 All roots correct! You can break words apart like a pro.

Grammar · Day 3
Writing Complete Sentences
✏️
Teacher models fixing fragments and run-ons

A complete sentence needs a subject and a verb and expresses a whole thought. A fragment is missing one of those pieces. A run-on jams two sentences together without proper punctuation.

👉 From the novel

“Kit could not read the faintest sign of welcome.” This is a complete sentence: Kit (subject) could not read (verb) a sign of welcome (complete thought).

🎯

Practice: Sentence Checker! Tap the label that correctly identifies each sentence as complete, fragment, or run-on.

Spelling · Day 3
Applying Syllable Types
🔠
Teacher reviews syllable types and models finding examples

You have learned the six syllable types. Today, put that knowledge to work by finding or writing your own example for each type from Chapter 3.

📌 Quick Reminder

Closed (CVC) — short vowel, ends in consonant: un-com-fort

Open (CV) — long vowel, ends in vowel: o-pen

VCe — silent e makes vowel long: home

Vowel team — two vowels together: reach

R-controlled — vowel + r: her

Consonant-le — ends in consonant + le: ta-ble

RWM — Read like a Reader, Read like a Writer, Mimic
“One hand reached to clutch the doorpost.”
📖 Read like a Reader
Who is doing this? Why? This is Rachel seeing Kit for the first time. What does this single action — clutching the doorpost — reveal about how Rachel feels in this moment?
✏️ Read like a Writer
Notice that the author shows one small physical action instead of telling us Rachel is shocked. How does this single detail create a stronger picture than saying “Rachel was surprised”?

What Good Readers Do
Analyze how figurative language contributes to meaning

Chapter 17 is the most figuratively rich chapter Nat has encountered in his own story. The moon is both a precise scientific instrument (for calculating longitude through lunar distances) and a source of something more — wonder, longing, the poetry of the sea. Good readers notice when an author uses figurative language not just for beauty but to say something true that plain language cannot.

🧠 Watch for this

When Latham describes the moon in Chapter 17, what figurative language does she use? Is the moon described as something cold and mathematical, something beautiful and poetic, or both at once?

How does Nat’s relationship to the moon differ from how a poet might see it? What does the moon mean to him?

What does it mean to say Nat uses the moon as a “clock in the sky”? What does this metaphor reveal about his way of thinking?

Morphology · Day 1
Prefixes trans-/inter-/sub-: Define
🎬
Coming soon

Three powerful prefixes help Nat describe the work of navigation — crossing oceans, finding positions between stars, and going beneath the surface of what a number means.

📌 The prefixes and what they mean

trans- — across, beyond, through: translate (carry meaning across languages), transport (carry across), transfer (carry across to another)

inter- — between, among: intersect (cut between), interval (space between), international (between nations)

sub- — under, below, beneath: submarine (under the sea), subtract (draw from under), subject (thrown under)

How navigators use these: Nat must translate star observations across into position calculations; he finds the interval between two measurements; he works beneath the surface complexity to find a submarine simplicity.

atlantic — crossing across the Atlantic Ocean
sect — to cut or cross between two lines
merge — to go under the surface of water
late — to carry meaning across from one language to another
🧭 All correct!

🔍 3-Step Decode: intersect

Step 1 — Context: When two lines of position intersect on a chart, the crossing point is the ship’s location. Nat used intersecting calculations from multiple stars to confirm a single precise position.

Step 2 — Word Parts: Inter- (between) + sect (cut, from Latin secare). To intersect is to cut between — to cross each other.

Step 3 — Confirm: Intersect means to cross at a point; to meet and cross. Two lines intersect at exactly one point unless they are parallel.

Grammar · Day 1
Relative Clauses: Define
🎬
Coming soon

A relative clause is a group of words that begins with a relative pronoun (who, which, that, whose, whom) and adds information about a noun. It expands a simple sentence into something richer and more precise.

📌 Relative clauses

Simple: Nat wrote a book. The book changed navigation forever.

Expanded: Nat wrote a book that changed navigation forever.

Simple: The sailors trusted Nat. He had corrected the almanac.

Expanded: The sailors trusted Nat, who had corrected the almanac.

Restrictive (no comma): that or who — the clause identifies which one: “The calculation that Nat used was more accurate.”

Non-restrictive (comma): which or who — the clause adds extra information: “The almanac, which contained hundreds of errors, had been trusted for years.”

Which sentence correctly uses a relative clause? Nat was a navigator, that corrected Moore's almanac. Nat was a navigator who corrected Moore’s almanac — a task that changed maritime history.
“The lunar observation, ___ Nat took at noon, confirmed the ship’s position.” — which relative pronoun fits? that — adds a restrictive clause (no comma needed) which — adds a non-restrictive clause (comma already there)

Spelling · Day 1
Assimilated Prefixes: Discover
🎬
Coming soon

When a prefix ends in a consonant and the root word starts with the same consonant, something interesting happens: the prefix often assimilates — it changes its last letter to match the root. This makes the word easier to say, but trickier to spell.

🔍 Assimilated prefixes

sub- + fix → suffix (not subfix) — the b becomes f to match

sub- + port → support (not subport) — the b becomes p to match

trans- + port → transport (no change — s before p is fine)

inter- + rupt → interrupt (no change needed)

The key insight: English spelling preserves the original prefix even when the sound changes. So support is spelled sup-port because sub assimilated to sup — but knowing the root sub helps you understand the word’s meaning.

🤔

In your reading journal, identify the hidden prefix in each word: suffix · support · subtract · interrupt · transport. Write: [assimilated prefix] + [root] = [full word].

BEFORE
Alex’s draft: Nat used lunar observations to find longitude at sea. This changed navigation.
AFTER
Alex’s clarified version: Nat mastered a technique called lunar-distance observation — measuring the precise angle between the moon and a known star to calculate the ship’s longitude, the east-west position that navigators could not otherwise determine at sea.

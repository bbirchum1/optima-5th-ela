Morphology · Introduction
Suffixes That Turn Verbs into Adjectives
🌱
Teacher introduces the suffixes -ive and -ative

The suffixes -ive and -ative turn verbs into adjectives. They mean “having the quality of” or “tending to.” When you add -ive or -ative to a verb, you get an adjective that describes a quality.

📌 Word families

create (verb) → creative (adjective) — having the quality of creating

instruct (verb) → instructive (adjective) — tending to instruct

imagine (verb) → imaginative (adjective) — having the quality of imagining

act (verb) → active (adjective) — tending to act

Notice: Kit is creative and imaginative — both -ative adjectives that describe someone who creates and imagines. These suffixes show up constantly in descriptions of character.

Vocabulary · Introduction
How to Figure Out Unfamiliar Words
🔤
Teacher models the 3-step vocabulary strategy

Chapter 9 introduces words connected to Kit’s first experience teaching. When you hit an unfamiliar word, use the three-step strategy: context, word parts, then confirm.

🔍 3-Step Decode: hornbook

Step 1 — Context: “They sat hunched on the bench, each holding in hand a hornbook, a small stout-handled wooden slab on which was fastened a tiny sheet of paper.” The sentence gives you a built-in definition — the author describes exactly what the object looks like.

Step 2 — Word Parts: Horn + book. The “horn” refers to a thin sheet of transparent animal horn that covered the paper to protect it. The “book” part tells you it was used for reading.

Step 3 — Confirm: A hornbook was a Colonial-era teaching tool — a wooden paddle with the alphabet and a prayer, covered by transparent horn. It was one of the few reading materials children had.

✏️ Grammar: Dialogue Punctuation & Paragraphing
Learn — define the rules of writing dialogue
✏️
Grammar · Introduction
How Writers Punctuate Dialogue
✏️
Teacher introduces dialogue punctuation rules

Chapter 9 is full of spoken dialogue. Speare follows clear punctuation rules every time a character speaks. Understanding these rules will help you both as a reader and a writer.

👉 Three dialogue rules from the novel

Rule 1 — Quotation marks: Put the spoken words inside quotation marks. “It is all my fault,” Kit faltered.

Rule 2 — Comma before the closing quote: When a said-tag follows, use a comma (not a period) inside the closing quotation mark. “I was just trying out a new idea,” she explained.

Rule 3 — New paragraph for a new speaker: Every time a different person starts talking, begin a new paragraph. This helps the reader track who is speaking.

Spelling · Introduction
The Schwa Sound — The Sneakiest Vowel
🔠
Teacher introduces schwa and reduced vowels

Look at these two groups of words. Say each word out loud. What do you notice about the bold syllable?

Group A — Schwa in the middle

imagine

different

separate

opposite

Say these aloud. Does the bold vowel sound clear, or does it almost disappear?

Group B — Schwa at the end

lesson

children

animal

Say these aloud. Can you hear the vowel in the last syllable, or does it become a lazy “uh” sound?

The schwa sound (written as /ə/) is the lazy “uh” sound that appears in unstressed syllables. It’s the most common vowel sound in English, but it’s tricky because any vowel letter (a, e, i, o, u) can make it.

Why it matters for spelling: You can’t hear which vowel letter to use — “separate” sounds like “sep-uh-rate,” not “sep-a-rate.” You have to memorize or use word families to know which letter is correct.

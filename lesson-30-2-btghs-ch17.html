Chapter 17 Question
What does it mean to call someone simply by their name?

In Chapter 17, Praiseworthy strikes gold and in his excitement calls Jack “Jack” — not “Master Jack.” Just Jack. Plain Jack — “the way he’d always wanted it to be.” This tiny moment — one word dropped — carries the weight of the entire novel.

📌 Editing Checklist: Punctuation, Capitalization & Clarity

Punctuation:

✓ Commas in compound sentences (before FANBOYS joining two independent clauses)

✓ Commas after introductory phrases and dependent clauses

✓ Apostrophes in contractions (it’s vs. its) and possessives (Jack’s)

✓ Dialogue punctuation: comma inside closing quote; new speaker = new paragraph

✓ Semicolons to join two closely related independent clauses

Capitalization:

✓ Proper nouns: names of people, places, ships, teams (Lady Wilma, San Francisco, Aunt Arabella)

✓ Titles before names (Captain Swain, Dr. Buckbee)

✓ First word of a sentence; first word of a direct quotation

Clarity:

✓ No run-on sentences — check for comma splices

✓ No dangling modifiers — the opening phrase must match the subject

✓ Consistent verb tense throughout a paragraph

Read: Chapter 17 — “The Fifteenth of August”
Gold, Gravediggers’ Hill, and the bout of the century
📌 Reading Focus

Chapter 17 is the joy chapter — the emotional peak of the whole novel. They find gold. They stake their claim with the tattered umbrella. They work for weeks. Then Praiseworthy is challenged to fight the Mountain Ox. He wins — not with brute strength, but with patience, footwork, and study of The Gentleman’s Book of Boxing. Both victories say the same thing: preparation and intelligence win.

⏸️ Pause & Think 1

In Chapter 17, Praiseworthy calls Jack simply “Jack” rather than “Master Jack.” Jack realizes this and thinks “the way he’d always wanted it to be.” Why does this tiny change in a name carry so much meaning?

📓
⏸️ Pause & Think 2

Praiseworthy wins the fight by dodging, jabbing, and studying the Mountain Ox’s patterns — not by overpowering him. Jack says afterward: “The Mountain Ox can hardly read or write.” What is Jack really saying about why Praiseworthy won?

📓
📖 Reading Question 1
They stake their claim using Praiseworthy’s tattered umbrella as a corner post. Trace the history of that umbrella through the novel — what has happened to it, and what does using it as a claim post symbolize about their journey?
📓
📖 Reading Question 2
Praiseworthy wins the boxing match by patience, footwork, study, and planning — not by outmuscling the Mountain Ox. How does this victory reflect the novel’s larger theme about the kind of intelligence Praiseworthy represents?
📓
📖 Reading Question 3
Chapter 17 is the climax of the novel. What combination of events and details makes this the emotional peak? Identify at least three specific moments and explain what each contributes to the feeling of triumph.

This is it — the final week. Your adventure story is ready to publish. Do one final editing pass using the checklist from Word Study. Then publish: write your final clean version (or type it), give it a title, and submit it. Every published piece deserves a title that captures its voice — not a description, but a phrase that makes a reader curious.

📋 Final Publishing Checklist

✓ My adventure has a beginning, middle, and end — all three sections are present

✓ My hero has a distinct voice — dialogue sounds like them, not like a narrator

✓ I have at least one specific, unexpected detail that only I would have put in

✓ My sentences have variety — short and long, different openers

✓ All dialogue is correctly punctuated

✓ I have a title that makes a reader curious

What Good Readers Do
Set your reading lens

Good readers pay attention to how an author introduces a new world. When you read a story set in an unfamiliar time and place, notice the details the author chooses — what people wear, what they say, how they react. These details help you understand not just the setting, but the kind of world the character is stepping into.

As you read Chapter 1, pay attention to the differences between Kit's old world and her new one. Ask yourself:

🧠 Watch for this

What details show Kit that Connecticut is very different from Barbados?

How does the author make you feel that difference?

What do the other characters' reactions tell you about the world Kit is entering?

Morphology · Introduction
Word Parts — Building Blocks of Meaning
🌱
Teacher introduces morphemes, bases, prefixes, and suffixes

Every word is built from smaller pieces called morphemes — the smallest units of meaning. A base word stands on its own. A prefix attaches before the base and changes the meaning. A suffix attaches after and often changes the part of speech.

Look at this word from Chapter 1: un-impress-ive. The prefix un- means "not." The base is impress. The suffix -ive turns the verb into an adjective. Together: "not impressive."

📌 Examples from the novel

un-impress-ive — prefix un- + base impress + suffix -ive (adjective)

dis-appoint-ing — prefix dis- + base appoint + suffix -ing (adjective/verb)

fortific-ation — base fortify + suffix -ation (noun)

re-spect-able — prefix re- + base spect + suffix -able (adjective)

Vocabulary · Introduction
How to Figure Out Unfamiliar Words
🔤
Teacher models the 3-step vocabulary strategy

Chapter 1 is full of nautical words — words connected to ships and sailing. When you hit an unfamiliar word, use this three-step strategy:

🔍 3-Step Decode: brigantine

Step 1 — Context: “The brigantine Dolphin left the open sea.” The word names the Dolphin. The Dolphin is sailing, so a brigantine must be a kind of ship.

Step 2 — Word Parts: The suffix -ine sometimes means "relating to." The root doesn't break down easily, but the context gave us a strong clue.

Step 3 — Confirm: A brigantine is a two-masted sailing ship. Context got us there.

Now try the remaining words on your own. Read the sentence from the novel. Use the context clues to figure out what the word might mean. Then tap the card to check.

Grammar · Introduction
What Makes a Sentence Complete?
✏️
Teacher introduces complete sentences, fragments, and run-ons

A complete sentence has a subject (who or what), a predicate (what the subject does), and expresses a complete thought. A fragment is missing one of those pieces. A run-on jams two complete sentences together without proper punctuation.

👉 From the novel

Complete sentence: “On a morning in mid-April, 1687, the brigantine Dolphin left the open sea.” Subject: the brigantine Dolphin. Predicate: left the open sea. Complete thought.

Fragment: “The bleak line of shore.” No verb — what is the shore doing? This is not a sentence.

Run-on: “Kit jumped into the water she rescued the doll.” Two complete thoughts fused together without a period or conjunction.

Spelling · Introduction
The Six Syllable Types
🔠
Teacher introduces the six syllable types

Look at these two groups of words. What patterns do you notice?

Group A

cap-stan • dol-phin • lum-ber

What do the bold syllables have in common?

Group B

bri-gan-tine • o-pen • mo-ment

What do the bold syllables have in common?

Group A syllables end in a consonant (closed syllables — the vowel makes a short sound). Group B syllables end in a vowel (open syllables — the vowel makes a long sound).

1. Closed — ends in a consonant, short vowel: cap, lum, dol

2. Open — ends in a vowel, long vowel: bri, o, mo

3. VCe (Vowel-Consonant-e) — silent e makes the vowel long: -tine in brigantine

4. Vowel Team — two vowels work together: sea, boat

5. R-Controlled — vowel + r changes the sound: har-bor, wa-ter

6. Consonant-le — final syllable with consonant + le: -tle in little

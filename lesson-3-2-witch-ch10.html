Daily Oral Language
Study the model, then fix the sentence

Sentence 1 — The Correct Model

Tap words you notice are correct. Watch for capitals, commas, and punctuation.

Hannah smiled and said, “There did well, child.”

Sentence 2 — Fix It

This sentence has mistakes. Rewrite it correctly in your notebook, using the model as a guide.

kit found hannahs cottage by blackbird pond and met a kind quaker woman

Corrected:

Kit found Hannah’s cottage by Blackbird Pond and met a kind Quaker woman.

Compare with the model: capitalize proper names (Kit, Hannah, Blackbird Pond) and the word Quaker (a specific religious group). “Hannah’s” needs an apostrophe to show possession.

Sentence 3 — Fix It

hannah tupper offered kit blueberry cake and told her about her husband thomas who had builded the house

Corrected:

Hannah Tupper offered Kit blueberry cake and told her about her husband, Thomas, who had built the house.

Capitalize proper names (Hannah Tupper, Kit, Thomas). Use “built” (correct past tense) instead of “builded.” Set off “Thomas” with commas because it’s an appositive renaming “her husband.”

🌱 Morphology: Suffixes -ive / -ative
Latin suffix · means “tending to” or “having the quality of”
🌱
Morphology · Practice
Spotting -ive and -ative Words
🌱
Teacher reviews -ive/-ative suffixes with chapter examples

The suffixes -ive and -ative come from Latin. They turn verbs into adjectives that mean “tending to” or “having the quality of.” This week, find these suffixes in context from Chapter 10.

📌 From Chapter 10

perceive → perceptive — Hannah has bright, perceptive eyes that notice Kit’s unhappiness

invite → inviting / invitative — Hannah’s cottage felt warm and inviting

create → creative — Kit found creative ways to visit Hannah in secret

attract → attractive — the meadow held an attractive peace for Kit

Find the -ive or -ative word in each sentence. Tap it!

“The cottage was small, but something about it felt protective, like a shelter from the world.”

“Kit had to be imaginative about finding excuses to visit Hannah without raising suspicion.”

“Nat was an active friend to Hannah, bringing her supplies from the ship.”

Grammar · Introduction
Identifying Dialogue Punctuation
✏️
Teacher models dialogue punctuation rules

When characters speak, their words need quotation marks, correct comma placement, and proper capitalization. Each new speaker gets a new paragraph.

👉 From the novel

“I need to be near the water,” Hannah said softly. — The comma goes inside the quotation marks, before the dialogue tag.

Hannah smiled. “Thomas Tupper built this house.” — When the tag comes first and ends with a period, the quote begins with a capital letter.

“There did well, child,” she said, “to come to the Meadow.” — An interrupted quote: comma inside the first set of marks, lowercase continues the sentence.

🎯

Practice: Grammar Spotter! For each sentence, tap whether the dialogue punctuation is Correct or Incorrect.

“Come in, child,” Hannah said warmly.

Correct Incorrect

Kit said “I feel so at home here”.

Correct Incorrect

“The swamp,” Hannah whispered, “was always beautiful to me.”

Correct Incorrect

“Nat brings me things from the ship.” Said Hannah.

Correct Incorrect

“Thomas built this house,” she told Kit. “He made it good and true.”

Correct Incorrect
🪶 Show What You Know · Grammar
Rewrite this passage with correct dialogue punctuation. Add quotation marks, commas, capitals, and paragraph breaks where needed:

Kit said I feel so at home here. Hannah smiled and replied There did well child to come to the Meadow.

Add quotation marks around the spoken words, commas before dialogue tags, and capitalize the first word of each quote. Start a new paragraph for each speaker.

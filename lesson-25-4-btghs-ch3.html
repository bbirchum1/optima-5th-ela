Poetry Corner
A poem about choices — and what it means to take the harder road

Robert Frost published this poem in 1916. It is one of the most famous in the English language — and one of the most misread. Most people think it celebrates taking the road “less traveled.” Read it carefully and you may find it’s about something more surprising: the stories we tell ourselves about our choices.

📜 Week Closing Poem
“The Road Not Taken”
Robert Frost, 1916 — public domain
Two roads diverged in a yellow wood,
And sorry I could not travel both
And be one traveler, long I stood
And looked down one as far as I could
To where it bent in the undergrowth;

Then took the other, as just as fair,
And having perhaps the better claim,
Because it was grassy and wanted wear;
Though as for that the passing there
Had worn them really about the same,

And both that morning equally lay
In leaves no step had trodden black.
Oh, I kept the first for another day!
Yet knowing how way leads on to way,
I doubted if I should ever come back.

I shall be telling this with a sigh
Somewhere ages and ages hence:
Two roads diverged in a wood, and I —
I took the one less traveled by,
And that has made all the difference.
💡

Here’s the surprising part: in stanzas 2 and 3, Frost says the two roads “had worn them really about the same.” They weren’t actually different. But by the last stanza, the speaker says he’ll claim he took “the one less traveled by.” This is a poem about how we construct stories about our choices after the fact. Captain Swain chooses to tow the fever ship. Praiseworthy chooses to remain a butler. Jack chooses the coal bunkers. Were these roads “less traveled” or just... the ones they took?

Letters home, a fever ship in need, and a race that matters more than winning
📌 Reading Focus

Chapter 3 gives you two things: Jack’s letter home (which reveals what he “forgot” to mention before, and hides what he doesn’t want Aunt Arabella to worry about), and Captain Swain’s choice (to tow the fever ship even though it could cost him the race). Look at both moments closely. They both say something about how people make choices when something more important than winning is at stake.

What Good Readers Do
Read the theme beneath the plot

Chapter 3 ends with Praiseworthy looking at the Southern Cross and saying, calmly, that he was “born to his calling” — born to be a butler, just like his father, and his father before him. But think about what he has done in three chapters: he’s survived hidden in a barrel, shoveled coal, outwitted a thief, and helped a ship in distress. A butler who does all of this isn’t unusual because of his job. He’s extraordinary because of his character. That’s what Fleischman is building toward: the idea that who you are is not the same as what your title says you are.

📖 Reading Question 1
In Chapter 3, Jack’s letter home reveals the full story of Cut-Eye Higgins — something he had previously described only as “a certain misfortune.” What specific details did Jack include in the letter that he hadn’t mentioned before? What does the way he tells the story reveal about his character?
📓
📖 Reading Question 2
When the fever ship calls for help, the Sea Raven runs while Captain Swain stops to tow them. What does the captain’s choice cost him? What does it tell us about the kind of person he is? Use evidence from Chapter 3 in your answer.
📓
📖 Reading Question 3 — Reflective (Chapters 1–3)
At the end of Chapter 3, Praiseworthy says he was “born to his calling” as a butler — yet almost nothing he has done in Chapters 1–3 is something a butler usually does. Based on everything you have read this week, what do you think Fleischman is saying about the relationship between a person’s role (their title or job) and their true ability (who they really are)? Use at least one specific detail from the chapters to support your thinking.

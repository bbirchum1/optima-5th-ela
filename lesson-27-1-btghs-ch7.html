📌 Two Latin Roots This Week

curr / curs (Latin) = run. Words with this root involve movement, flow, or the passage of time.

tract (Latin) = pull, drag. Words with this root involve drawing something toward, or pulling it along.

📌 Examples

current — water (or events) running continuously

occur — to run into; to happen

curriculum — a course (a running route for learning)

excursion — a running out; a short journey outward

precursor — something that runs before; a forerunner

tractor — something that pulls; a machine for pulling/hauling

attract — to pull toward; to draw interest

distract — to pull away from; to divert attention

extract — to pull out; how gold is removed from the earth

contract — to pull together; a binding agreement

💡

Connect to the novel: The whole voyage is a current of events rushing forward. The Sea Raven tries to distract Captain Swain with its coal theft. When Praiseworthy notices the lumber, he helps the captain extract a solution. And the race itself is driven by a contract — Swain wins the race, he gets the new clipper ship.

ex — to pull out; how gold is removed from rock
preor — something that runs before; a forerunner
at — to pull toward; to draw interest or people
oc — to happen; to run into (as in "when it occurs")
re — to pull back; to take back a statement
🌱 All correct!

📌 Compound & Complex Sentences for Action

Compound sentence: Two independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so — FANBOYS) or a semicolon. Equal ideas, forward momentum.
Example: “The Sea Raven passed with a blast of her whistle, and the gold-seekers on the Lady Wilma fell silent.”

Complex sentence: An independent clause + a dependent clause (because, when, although, unless, while, since, until, if). Shows cause, condition, or time relationship.
Example: “Because the Sea Raven had loaded too much coal, she sat low in the water — and the Lady Wilma crept ahead.”

In action writing: Compound = simultaneous events happening together. Complex = cause and effect, the engine of adventure.

📌 The –tion / –sion Suffix Family

The suffix –tion or –sion turns a verb into a noun meaning the act of or the state of:

extract → extraction · distract → distraction · construct → construction

excurse → excursion · attract → attraction · direct → direction

confuse → confusion · tense → tension

Spelling note: Use –sion after most words ending in –s, –se, or –d. Use –tion after most other bases. When in doubt, say it aloud — the sound tells you: /shun/ is usually –tion; /zhun/ is usually –sion.

Writer’s Workshop · Week 27 · BUILD THE MIDDLE
The race is won — now your hero needs a new obstacle
✓ Plan ✓ Draft ⭐ Build the Middle Revise & Edit Publish
💡

This week: raise the stakes. You have a hero and a scene. Now make things harder. The middle of every great adventure is where things go wrong, unexpected, or impossible. Fleischman’s secret: each chapter ends with a new problem, not a solution. Chapter 7 ends with the race still undecided. Chapter 8 ends with no money. Chapter 9 ends with Cut-Eye Higgins on the same stagecoach. That momentum is your model.

📋 Build the Middle: Three Questions

1. What goes wrong for your hero that they didn’t expect?

2. What do they try — and how does that make things even harder?

3. What is the moment of greatest pressure before they find a way forward?

Write the middle section of your adventure. Make things worse before they get better.

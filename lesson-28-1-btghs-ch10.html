📌 Two Latin Roots This Week

fort (Latin fortis) = strong. Words with this root deal with strength — physical or of character.

val / valid (Latin valere) = to be strong or to be worth. Words with this root deal with strength, worth, or value.

📌 Examples

fortify — to make strong; to strengthen defenses

fortitude — strength and courage in bearing difficulty

fortress — a strong, protected place

comfort — to make strong again; to ease someone’s pain

effort — a putting-out of strength; exertion

valor — great bravery; being strong in the face of danger

valid — strong, sound, legally defensible; not weak or false

validate — to confirm as true or strong; to prove worth

prevail — to be stronger than; to win out

evaluate — to determine the worth of something

equivalent — equal in value or worth

💡

Connect to the novel: Praiseworthy demonstrates fortitude throughout the voyage. His valor peaks when he punches the ruffian in Chapter 10. The whole story tests whether hope and effort can prevail against long odds.

🌱 All correct!

📌 Abstract Noun Endings: –ity, –ment, –ness

These three suffixes turn adjectives (or sometimes verbs) into abstract nouns — nouns naming a quality, state, or result rather than a thing you can see or touch.

–ity: transforms adjectives into nouns of quality or degree.
valid → validity · brave → bravity · curious → curiosity

–ment: transforms verbs into nouns of result or process.
amaze → amazement · achieve → achievement · establish → establishment

–ness: transforms adjectives into nouns of state or condition.
bold → boldness · clever → cleverness · dark → darkness

Spelling notes: –ity often requires a spelling change to the base (brave → bravity? No — bravery uses –ery, but many follow the pattern). –ment and –ness attach directly to most base words. –ity often drops a final –e: curious → curiosity, creative → creativity.

✏️

📌 Transitions & Introductory Phrases

These words and phrases connect ideas in literary response writing. They tell the reader how one idea relates to the next.

However — contrast or limit an idea
Therefore — show a logical conclusion
Furthermore — add a related point
Meanwhile — show simultaneous events
Nevertheless — contrast despite what came before
Consequently — show a result or effect
In contrast — set two things against each other
As a result — show what followed from an action

Punctuation rule: When these begin a sentence or clause, they are followed by a comma: However, Praiseworthy remained calm. When they connect two independent clauses, use a semicolon before and a comma after: The ruffians held the stage; nevertheless, Praiseworthy did not flinch.

✏️

In your journal: Write all eight transition words/phrases, then sort them into two categories: (1) Contrast or limit (however, nevertheless, in contrast) and (2) Add, conclude, or show effect (therefore, furthermore, meanwhile, consequently, as a result). Write one sentence for each category explaining the difference.

Read: Chapter 10 — “The Rogue Out-Rogued”
Cut-Eye Higgins, a highway robbery, and the picture of Aunt Arabella
📌 Reading Focus

Chapter 10 shows Praiseworthy at his most resourceful. The threat is real: Cut-Eye Higgins has a pistol. Then the stagecoach is stopped by highwaymen. Praiseworthy uses every tool he has — calm, wit, timing, and finally fury — to protect Jack and their grubstake. Watch how he handles each threat differently, and ask yourself: what reveals character more — how someone acts in danger, or what makes them finally lose their composure?

⏸️ Pause & Think 1

Cut-Eye Higgins sits across from Jack with his hand resting on a pistol. Praiseworthy pokes Jack gently and then takes a nap. What does this response tell us about Praiseworthy’s strategy?

📓
⏸️ Pause & Think 2

When the outlaw demands Praiseworthy remove his gloves, Praiseworthy does so “carefully but casually.” How does this tiny detail save everything?

📓
📖 Reading Question 1
When Cut-Eye Higgins is on the stagecoach, Praiseworthy remains completely calm — he even introduces Higgins to the undertaker by name. What is his strategy, and how does it work?
📓
📖 Reading Question 2
During the highway robbery, Praiseworthy removes his gloves “carefully but casually” to show he has no rings — while the gold dust stays inside the glove fingers. How does this one act reveal both his intelligence and his self-control?
📓
📖 Reading Question 3
Praiseworthy is calm during everything — Cut-Eye Higgins, the armed robbery, the outlaw searching the carpetbag. Then a ruffian takes Aunt Arabella’s picture, and Praiseworthy strikes. What does this moment reveal about where his true courage comes from? What does the picture represent?

💡

This week: shift to literary response. You’ve been writing your own adventure story. Now you write about Fleischman’s. A literary response makes a claim about theme — what Fleischman is saying about courage, resourcefulness, loyalty, or hope — and supports it with specific evidence from the text.

📋 Literary Response Structure

Opening: Name the book, author, and your theme claim.
"In By the Great Horn Spoon!, Sid Fleischman suggests that true courage is not the absence of fear — it is acting well in spite of it."

Body: Two or three pieces of evidence from the text, each explained. Use transitions: However, Furthermore, As a result, In contrast.

Closing: Restate what Fleischman is teaching — and why it matters.

📌 Two Latin Roots This Week

aud (Latin) means hear. When you see this root, the word usually has something to do with listening or sound.

oral/or (Latin) means speak or mouth. When you see this root, the word usually has something to do with speaking, words, or the mouth.

📌 Examples in Action

audible — able to be heard (an audible sound is one you can actually hear)

audience — a group of people listening (those who hear a performance)

audition — a test in which someone is heard performing

oral — spoken, not written; using the mouth

oration — a formal speech; an act of speaking

oracle — someone who speaks wisdom or prophecy

orator — a skilled public speaker

💡

Connect it to the novel: Praiseworthy announces everything — he makes formal speeches, gives commands, delivers news with perfect calm. The Gold Rush itself spread because of word of mouth: people heard the news and went racing west. Aud and or are the roots of how the adventure even started.

expedition = a journey undertaken for a specific purpose, especially a long or organized one.
🔍 Word 2: argonaut
1
Read the sentence from Chapter 1
“Argonauts of old, they are, chasing after the golden fleece,” roared Captain Swain, his brass buttons glowing in the firelight.
2
Look at word history
In Greek myth, Jason and his crew sailed on the ship Argo to find the Golden Fleece. His sailors were called Argo + nauts (sailors). By 1849, newspapers began calling Gold Rush seekers “Argonauts” — because they too were sailing for gold.
3
Confirm the meaning
argonaut = originally, a sailor on Jason’s ship in Greek myth; in 1849, a nickname for any California Gold Rush seeker who came by sea.

📌 What is a Prepositional Phrase?

A prepositional phrase begins with a preposition (a word that shows relationship — in, on, under, through, around, at, of, by, with, from, toward) and ends with a noun or pronoun. It tells us where, when, how, or in what way something happens.

From the novel:

“In the creaking darkness of her cargo hold, there sat eighteen barrels of potatoes.”

“A sailing ship with two great sidewheels went splashing out of Boston harbor.”

Notice how the prepositional phrases paint the scene — they put you in the darkness, they put the ship in the harbor. Without them, the setting disappears.

Read: The California Gold Rush
Build the background — what world is Jack entering?

Our story begins on a very specific day: January 27, 1849. Read this passage to understand exactly what was happening in America at that moment — and why Jack’s wild plan wasn’t as crazy as it sounds.

📜 Background Knowledge Passage

The Discovery That Changed Everything

On January 24, 1848, a carpenter named James Marshall spotted something glinting in a streambed at Sutter’s Mill near Coloma, California. It was gold. His boss, John Sutter, tried to keep the discovery quiet — but within weeks, word leaked out. By the end of 1848, news of the California gold strike had swept across the United States and around the world. President James K. Polk confirmed it publicly in December 1848, and the nation erupted.

The Rush Begins: Who Were the Forty-Niners?

The people who flooded into California starting in 1849 became known as the Forty-Niners, named for the year they arrived. By the end of 1849, approximately 80,000 fortune-seekers had reached California. By 1853, that number would climb to 250,000. They came from every state in the Union and from dozens of foreign countries: China, Chile, France, Australia, Mexico, Ireland. About 95 percent were men. Most left their families at home with a promise: I’ll come back rich.

Three Routes West

Getting to California was nearly as dangerous as the gold fields themselves. There were three main routes, and none was easy.

The first route was the Oregon-California Trail, a grueling 2,000-mile overland journey through plains, deserts, and mountains. The wagons rolled from Missouri toward Sacramento. The trip took roughly six months, and cholera — a deadly disease — killed thousands along the way.

The second route cut across Central America. Travelers sailed from the East Coast to Panama, trekked through dense jungle to the Pacific side, and boarded another ship north to San Francisco. This route was shorter but notoriously dangerous: yellow fever and malaria lurked in the jungle, and ships on the Pacific side were desperately overbooked.

The third route was the longest: the Cape Horn sea voyage. Ships left the harbors of Boston or New York, sailed south down the entire Atlantic coast of South America, rounded the treacherous southern tip of the continent at Cape Horn, and then turned north up the Pacific toward San Francisco. The voyage covered roughly 18,000 nautical miles and took four to six months. Ships were badly overcrowded. Passengers faced rough seas, spoiled food, and illness. And yet for those on the East Coast, it was often the most popular choice — it was the route they knew, the route by ship.

Life in the Gold Fields

The reality of gold mining was usually harsh. Most Forty-Niners panned for gold in streams using a metal pan, swirling muddy water until heavier gold particles settled to the bottom. The work was backbreaking, the camps were muddy, prices for everything were outrageous, and most miners found little or nothing. The ones who often made the most reliable fortunes were the merchants, doctors, lawyers, and skilled tradespeople who supplied the miners with what they needed.

Still, the dream was real. In 1849 alone, about $10 million worth of gold was pulled from California’s streams. In 1852, at the peak of the Rush, that number jumped to $81 million. For every person who went home empty-handed, there were stories — real stories — of men who picked up nuggets the size of goose eggs.

That dream is what sent Jack Flagg and Praiseworthy into those potato barrels. And it’s what sent 80,000 others toward the same improbable gamble.

This week’s writing: Over the next six weeks, you will write your own adventure story — inspired by Jack and Praiseworthy, but completely your own. Today you plan the foundation: your hero, your problem, and your setting. Great adventures don’t begin with a plot — they begin with a character who wants something desperately and a world that makes it hard to get.

👀 Watch How — Meet Alex, our example writer

Alex wants to write an adventure story about a girl who stows away on a research vessel heading to Antarctica. Here’s Alex’s plan:

Hero
Maya, 12. She has spent her whole life reading her scientist mother’s journals. She is brilliant and reckless and terrified of failure.
Problem / Goal
Maya’s mother’s research station is in danger of losing funding. The only way to save it is to find proof of the ice formation her mother always claimed existed.
Setting
A research ship crossing the Southern Ocean toward Antarctica. Cold, remote, dangerous. The crew doesn’t know she’s on board.
What’s at stake
If she fails, the station closes. If she’s discovered, she’s sent home. Both feel like losing everything.

Notice: Alex doesn’t have a plot yet — just the pieces that will make a plot possible. The setting creates pressure. The hero has a specific goal. The stakes are clear. Now Alex can start drafting.
